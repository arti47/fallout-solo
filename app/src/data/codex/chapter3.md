# Chapter 3: CHARACTER CREATION

The first step in telling your first Wasteland story in Wasteland Wanderer is to create a Vault Dweller, your persona in Fallout, and the viewpoint from which you will experience your adventure.

Creating a character in Fallout: Wasteland Wanderer is a simple process, broken down into seven stages-at the end of which, you will have a filled-in Pip-Boy Sheet and all you need to start your adventure.

These steps are:

1. **Create your Vault** Add your Vault to your Map Grid; determine what happened there and how it affected your character.
2. **Set your S.P.E.C.I.A.L. Attributes** Determine your character's starting S.P.E.C.I.A.L. Attributes. Add S.P.E.C.I.A.L. Attributes to your character tab.
3. **Set your Skills** Determine your character's starting Tag Skills and overall Skill Ranks. Add Skills to your character tab.
4. **Calculate Health and Luck Points** Determine your character's maximum Hit and Luck Points. Add HP and LP to your character tab.
5. **Select starting Equipment** Gain Equipment according to your character's skills. Add your character's starting equipment to your Inventory Tab.
6. **Character Description** Name your character and add small finishing touches. Add your character's name to your character Tab.
7. **Create your Main Quest** Generate your character's Main Quest, the backbone of their story. Add a Main Quest to your Data Tab.

Once the seventh step is complete, you will be ready to play! Remember that you will need a Pip-Boy Sheet to complete Character Creation, which can be photocopied from or printed from the Fallout: Wasteland Wanderer Downloads page at Modiphius.com.

## STAGE 1: CREATE YOUR VAULT

One thing connects all Vault Dwellers, their time in their namesake Vault. Almost every Vault created by the eponymous Vault-Tec Corporation was created with some form of ulterior motive; an experiment, study, or even long-term business venture-each as varied as the numbers marking the Vault Door. This motive, no matter what it was, affected those who once called it home, and in this step, you will determine not only where your character's Vault is but what it is, who else called it home, and how it affected them. To create your character's Home Vault, follow the presented Six Steps in order to determine the Vault's Experiment, Population, the character's Starting Reputation, any Linked NPCs, and finally, the Vault Number and Region.

### STEP 1: EXPERIMENT

Almost every Vault created by Vault-Tec housed some form of experiment, some chemical, others social, others just for fun. In this step, you will determine the experiment that your character's Vault was subjected to. To do so, roll a die on the Vault Experiment Table and read the associated entry. This will outline the experiment that your character has been exposed to; once read, add the listed Truths to the Vault Truths section on your Map Tab.

**What is a Truth?**
Readers familiar with the Modiphius 2d20 Line of TTRPGs will be familiar with the idea of Truths; for those new to them, however, the natural question to ask is, "What is a Truth?" Simply put, a Truth is a short descriptor of a location, NPC, or item and can range from a generalized concept to a specific fact, a method of quickly understanding a core feature of what makes something what it is. For example, Vault 101 (from Fallout 3) may have the Truths 'Isolated, Rad-roach Infestation, Isolationist'. These Truths help quickly understand the Vault, its purpose, and the people within. Truths have a minor impact on gameplay but are mostly used to justify your decisions when asking The Oracle or filling out your journal.

**Inspiration**
The Vault Experiment Table, as well as most of the information presented in this book, are, in fact, as much inspiration as they are hard and fast rules to how to run your own game of Wasteland Wanderer. Should you roll on a table and be struck by a burst of inspiration, run with it and bring your own spark to the story-never limit your imagination by the constraints of a randomized roll table!

### STEP 3: REPUTATION

Before leaving the Vault, your character lived a life, and that life touched those who also call the Vault home (unless the 'One, Including You' result was rolled on the Vault Population Table). In this step, we will determine your character's Reputation within their home Vault. Roll on the Reputation Table to determine your character's Vault Reputation, adding the details to your Map Tab. After rolling, take a moment to think about what the event that caused this Reputation was and what it means for your character. You do not need to enter this onto your Pip-Boy Sheets, but it could be your first entry in your notepad or recorder-this step on your character's journey may be the reason they left their home in the first place!

**What is Reputation?**
In game, Reputation is used to quickly remind yourself how NPCs from a noted community will generally act toward you. By completing quests for a settlement, they may change their opinion toward you for the better! Conversely, damaging a settlement may negatively affect their opinion, making your character's Reputation decline.

With your character's Reputation determined, you can now create your first NPC in Step 4, one linked to your character!

## STAGE 2: SET YOUR S.P.E.C.I.A.L ATTRIBUTES

As we all know from the Vault-Tec reels, we are all S.P.E.C.I.A.L., and in this stage, we will determine exactly how S.P.E.C.I.A.L. your Vault Dweller is! By the end of this stage, you will assign points to each of your character's seven Attributes, which are Strength, Perception, Endurance, Charisma, Intelligence, Agility, and Luck. Each of these Attributes is ranked from 1-10; the higher the number, the greater the character's affinity with that Attribute. A character with a Strength of 10 will be known through the Wasteland for their incredible feats of brawn, whereas the only tales told about someone with a Strength of 1 will be about how they once got outwrestled by a Radroach.

### ATTRIBUTE DISTRIBUTION

Before assigning any Attribute points, each Attribute starts with a value of 4. Note this, and then distribute 12 additional Attribute points, raising the selected scores as you see fit. No Attribute can be raised above a value of 10 at this stage. Whether you favor balance over specialization or prefer to focus your character on a singular purpose, you can create any type of character you wish. A preassigned spread of points has been demonstrated here, so you can see what a balanced, focused, or specialized character may look like:

- Balanced: 6, 6, 6, 6, 6, 5, 5
- Focused: 8, 7, 6, 6, 5, 4, 4
- Specialized: 9, 8, 5, 5, 5, 4, 4

**What makes you S.P.E.C.I.A.L.?**
Each Attribute has its own usage, and here you will find a short description of each and why you may want to use it!

**Strength (STR)**
Strength measures a character's physical prowess. It is used by characters to make melee attacks, use Heavy Weapons, and exert themselves beyond their limits. Buy ranks in the Strength Attribute if you want to solve problems through brute force.

**Perception (PER)**
Perception influences how easily a character notices danger, how aware they are of their environment, and how it is used to make attacks with Energy weapons. Buy ranks in the Perception Attribute if you want your character to be an observant person and solve problems by studying your surroundings.

**Endurance (END)**
Endurance represents natural toughness and resilience. It influences how many Health Points a character has and how easy they find it to shrug off physical dangers. Buy ranks in the Endurance Attribute if you want your character to be hardy and resilient, and they push through problems rather than solve them.

**Charisma (CHA)**
Charisma influences how effectively a character can convince people of their opinions, change their minds, barter, and find safety among others. Buy ranks in the Charisma Attribute if you want your character to be persuasive and solve problems by talking their way out of them.

**Intelligence (INT)**
Intelligence measures a character's smarts, how able they are to apply their mind to an obstacle, and how much they remember and know from studying the world around them. Buy ranks in the Intelligence Attribute if you want your character to be cunning and clever and solve your problems by thinking through the most effective solution.

**Agility (AGI)**
Agility measures how fit and agile a character is, their balance, athleticism, and how precisely they can control their body. It is used to make ranged attacks with Small Guns. Buy ranks in the Agility Attribute if you want your character to be nimble and solve problems with accuracy and skill.

**Luck (LCK)**
Luck measures just how much fortune smiles on a character, how much random chance falls in their favor, and generally how often they come out on top against all the odds. Buy ranks in Luck if you always want your character to have the upper hand on their surroundings, avoid dangerous scenarios, and rarely be caught out.

Once you have decided how to spend your Attribute points, note them in the Attributes section of your Data Tab, and you're ready to move onto Stage 4: Set your Skills.

## STAGE 3: SET YOUR SKILLS

Before leaving their Vault, your character had developed a unique set of skills-either as part of their experiment, due to their job, or even just a hobby; now free to walk the Wasteland, they get to put those skills to the test in a completely new environment. In this stage, you will be either picking a Career Template to assign to your character, which will determine their Skills, or assigning them yourself. Each Skill has a rank from 0-5; as with Attributes, the higher the number, the greater that character's affinity with the Skill. For example, a character with a Small Guns rating of 5 would be well known for their ability to shoot the wings off a bloatfly at 40 paces-whereas one with a rating of 0 may even struggle to understand how to load a pistol. There are two methods to select your character's skills: selecting a Career Template by taking the G.O.A.T. or Assigning your Skills.

### ASSIGN YOUR SKILLS

An alternative to selecting a Career Template is to Assign your own Skills. To do so, assign a total of 15 (+ your character's Intelligence attribute) Ranks across the available skills in an array of your choice, though no skill may be raised over 5 Ranks during character creation. Once the Ranks have been assigned, select two skills to become Tag Skills.

**Tag Skills**
Tag Skills in the Fallout games are Skills important to your character that gain a small bonus during character creation. In Wasteland Wanderer, Tag Skills represent a similar mindset-with each Tag Skill representing a Skill your character has focused on developing. This manifests in Wasteland Wanderer by a Tag skill having an increased Critical Range, meaning you are more likely to roll multiple successes when making a Skill Test in that Skill. Critical Range, Skill Tests, and Successes are discussed more in Chapter 4: How to Play.

## STAGE 4: CALCULATE HEALTH AND LUCK POINTS

In the Wasteland, your physical well-being is as tied to luck as it is to any skills or knowledge you possess; you can be as prepared and trained as you like, but it means very little if you wander into a Deathclaw nest by accident. The same is true for your character in Wasteland Wanderer, and in this stage, we shall populate your Stats Tab with your character's Level, Maximum Health Points, and Maximum Luck Points.

**LEVEL**
In Wasteland Wanderer, your character begins play at Level 1; ensure that the Level section of your Stats Tab is marked to show this. You will increase your character's level during play.

**MAXIMUM HEALTH POINTS**
Your character's health points (HP) measure how tough they are and how much Damage it takes to knock them out or kill them. Their maximum Health Points are equal to 5 + END. On your Stats Tab, write down your character's maximum HP above the HP Track (the series of boxes). This track represents your character's vitality and ability to take Damage.

**MAXIMUM LUCK POINTS**
Luck points are a spendable resource distinct from your LCK Attribute that can be spent and regained throughout play. The maximum number of Luck points you can have at any time is equal to half your LCK Attribute rounded up. On your Stats Tab, write down your character's maximum LCK Points above the Luck Track (the series of boxes). This track represents your character's remaining Luck. Once these above sections have been filled, you have completed this stage and can move to Stage 5: Select Starting Equipment.

## STAGE 5: SELECT STARTING EQUIPMENT

A character's equipment is just as important as their skills; these are the tools they will rely on to not just complete their Quests but survive from day to day. It's a tough place out there, and those with only the clothes on their back and an old knife tend to not last long. To select your character's starting equipment, choose two Skills that have at least 3 ranks on your Stats Tab, and add the equipment listed in the Skill Item Table that is tied to those two Skills to your Inventory Tab. Then add two Supplies to your Supplies Track; once this is done, you move to Step 6: Character Description.

### EQUIPMENT AND ENCUMBRANCE

Equipment represents physical items your character has and can be used when making tests (Skill Tests ). Equipment listed with a quantity number (i.e., x3 Stimpaks) is used upon use, meaning that you have a limited supply. Equipment not marked in such a way (A Flamer) will last until it is damaged through a story event. Using equipment creatively is a key element to surviving the Wasteland. If you're looking for a more realistic experience, restrict the amount of equipment your character can carry by using common sense, taking a look at what they are carrying, and imagining how they achieve that. Otherwise, you may prefer to stick to an abstract system where your inventory is mostly ignored until you use it. Either approach works, and it is up to you to decide how you want to handle encumbrance.

## STAGE 7: SET UP YOUR MAIN QUEST

Now that you know everything about your character, it's time to determine their reason for heading into the Wasteland, their Main Quest. The focus of your character's story will revolve around this Main Quest, their time in the Wasteland will be led by this, and the manner in which their lives will change because of it. To generate a Main Quest, you need a Goal, a Blocker, and a Location where that Main Quest is situated, and you will generate each of these by the end of this stage, after which you will be ready to begin your story!

### GOAL

The first step to creating a Main Quest is to create an overarching goal and write it onto your Data Tab. This Goal should be something that will take a lot of effort to accomplish-no one's interested in a story about fetching the milk! A Goal is the living heart of your character's journey; it's the main reason they left their Vault. Without this, the story would never have begun, so take your time considering it. You can either come up with your own or generate a result from the Goal Table. Once you have decided on a goal, you should then take some time to consider why your character wants to achieve it, what drives you, and what you risk if this goal is not achieved? Note down both your Goal in the Main Quest section of your Data Tab and any additional notes in the Main Quest Notes section. Once this is done, you generate your Blocker.

### BLOCKER

Next, you must decide what the most important and immediate problem you face in completing your goal. Again, you can either choose a logical Blocker for your goal or roll on the Blocker Table for inspiration. The Blocker is the thing stopping your character from achieving their goal, a significant threat or obstacle that actively acts as a stop point for your character. No story is complete without opposition, and every protagonist requires an antagonist--so when determining your Blocker, also think about why that Blocker exists and how it interacts with the goal itself. Blockers will apply to different Goals in different ways; for example, rolling the Politics Blocker will be characterized vastly differently depending on the goal. Should the goal be A Cure, then perhaps the two Factions are arguing over how best to use it, whereas if the goal were Destabilized then perhaps one of the Factions is responsible for the assassination-but both Settlements are blaming the other. Think about the implications of the Blocker and how it will best drive your narrative forward.

### BLOCKER LOCATION
The final step in creating your Main Quest is determining where it is; this step is the simplest but by no means the least important. The Blocker Location dictates where your Goal is. And what you need to overcome to reach it. It also creates the baseline of your character's journey. Location is as important as the Blocker in Wasteland Wanderer, as most of Fallout focuses not just on the destination but on the journey there. If the Wasteland Wanderer had stumbled out of Vault 111 and then met their father in Megaton, there simply wouldn't have been much to that story. After all, it is called the hero's journey.

To determine the Blocker Location, roll a D20 until you get a result that matches the number associated with a Location on the outer edge of your Map; that Location then becomes your Blocker Location.

**How do I Complete my Main Quest?**
Each time you reach a Blocker Location during your gameplay, you will complete an Encounter tied to that Blocker; once that is completed, you will be able to make a Clear Main Quest Blocker Action, which will determine if you are able to attempt your final step, or if another Blocker has appeared in your way. Once no Blockers remain, you can attempt to finish your Main Quest and close out your story.

### ADVANCEMENT
During your character's story, they will gain Experience Points (XP) whenever they complete a Quest; XP is a way of showing the character's growth on their adventure their ability to adapt and change as a person, increasing their likelihood of both survival and success. Upon the completion of a Side Quest, your character will gain 1 XP, and at the end of a Main Quest, they will gain 3. This XP is then listed on your Data Tab and can be spent by taking the Level Up Action. Each time the Level Up Action is taken, you will be able to pick a new Perk your character meets the prerequisites for, which is then added to your Data Tab. From that moment on, your character gains the Perks bonus, becoming better for all the trials and tribulations the Wasteland has dolled out to them.

**PREREQUISITES**
Each Perk has a prerequisite, which is a requirement that the character must meet to be able to take the Perk in the first place. If your character does not meet the prerequisites, then they cannot take the Perk.

**Advancement Example**
Ben's Vault Dweller 'Nicholas Rose' has just finished a Side Quest, and he has decided that after a hard day, Nick is going to kick up his feet, pop a Nuka-Cola, and Level Up. Ben removes the 1XP he has gained from the Side Quest from his Data Tab and then has a look over the available Perks to decide what he will buy. Nick has been in a lot of fights recently, and Ben wants to ensure that Nick lives to see the end of his Main Quest-so he has a look over the Perks and finds two that appeal to him; Adamantine Skeleton and Dodger, so he checks the prerequisites.

Adamantine Skeleton has a prerequisite of END 6, whereas Dodger has a prerequisite of AGL 6. Nick has an END of 9 and an AGL of 5; therefore, he doesn't meet the requirements of Dodger, but he does meet the requirements for Adamintine Skeleton. As he can only pick one of the two, Ben picks Adamintine Skeleton and writes it into the Perks Section of his Data Tab, and Nick leans back, sips on his Nuka-Cola, and feels like he's just that little bit more secure in the stability of his bones.
