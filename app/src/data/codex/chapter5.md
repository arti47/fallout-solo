 Encounter step.

TRAVEL

The near-endless roads of America are as
 much a part of it as the people who walk
 them.
In the Wasteland, the only thing
 that has changed is the state of those
 roads.
Every story in the Fallout series is a
 story about Travel, about the Vault Dweller
 venturing out to find a water chip, the
 Courier walking the Mojave to enter New
 Vegas;
each time the journey was as, if not
 more important, than the destination.
So,
 it's only right that in Wasteland Wanderer,
 you begin each round of play by putting
 dirt under your boots and walking the
 Wasteland.
The first step of a Round is the Travel
 step, where you pick your character's next
 destination on the Map, before determin-
 ing what they find there, and encounter
 along the way.
Travel is broken down into
 four Steps: Pick a Location, Determine
 Inhabitants, Generate Icons and Truths,
 and finally, Flesh it Out.
If you are trav-
 elling to a new Location, you complete all
 three parts, but if you have travelled to a
 previously visited Location, you only need
 to complete the first.
At the beginning of your story, your char-
 acter will have just left their Vault, meaning
 that they inhabit the same location as the
 Vault-which should be the central loca-
 tion on your Map Tab.
Now come on. Let's hit the road.
 -Piper Wright

Tracking your Location
 When using your Map Tab, it's important to
 remember where your character is.
At the
 start of your story, this will be easy, as there
 will only be a few locations where they could
 be, but as the tale goes on and the Map
 opens up, it will become harder.
Therefore,
 it is recommended that you find a way of
 tracking that location that works best for you.
A few methods could be:

■ Write your current Location in your Journal.
■Note your current Location with an easily
 erased pencil mark.
If your Map Tab is in a stable location, mark
 your Location with a token of some kind.
PICK A LOCATION

The first step of Travel is determining
 where you want to go.
To do this, pick a
 Location adjacent to your current location
 on the Map Tab (diagonal locations are not
 adjacent) and determine if it's Unexplored
 or Explored.
This process is as simple as
 checking your Map Tab and seeing if you
 have populated any information about
 that location yet;
if the answer is no, the
 location is Unexplored, otherwise it is
 Explored.

■ Explored: Spend 1 Supply to Travel;
if
 you cannot, Lose 2HP. Then Travel to
 the selected Location and generate an
 Encounter.

■ Unexplored: Spend 1 Supply to Travel;
 if you cannot, Lose 2HP.
Then Travel to
 the selected Location and Determine
 Inhabitants.
When you are choosing a Location, you
 cannot elect to remain where you are

already;
as there is no limit to the number
 of Actions you can take after an Encounter
 in a Location, you should not need to
 remain in one space.

Impassable Locations

Some locations are Impassable; these can
 be identified by the fact that they do not
 have a location number.
You cannot enter
 these locations for any reason, though you
 may decide that you find a tunnel or similar,
 allowing you to pass through them-it's up
 to you!
Is Home even Home
 anymore?

For the purposes of determining if a Location
 is near a Settlement, your Starting Vault is not
 considered a Settlement.
If you are Traveling to an Explored
 Location, you now generate an Encounter.
 Otherwise, Progress to Determine
 Inhabitants.

When entering an Unexplored Location,

Whenever you Travel to an Unexplored

To do so, you roll a d20 to [GENERATE

■Wasteland: If a Wasteland is generated,

■ Settlement: If a Settlement is gener-

 either select, or generate a [FACTION]

GENERATE ICONS AND TRUTHS

No two places in the Wasteland are quite
 alike, that's as true in the Fallout games as
 it is in Wasteland Wanderer.
Once you've
 determined if a location is inhabited, it's
 time to determine the Icons and Truths of
 that location and what marks it out from
 the other spaces in the Wasteland.
ICONS

Icons are landmarks, places within a loca-
 tion that are iconic in one way or another;
the type of place that others would use to
 note the location apart from another one.
For example, the space around Tenpenny
 Tower is relatively unimpressive; it's the
 enormous tower that singles it out.
An Icon
 is a great starting-off point for listing inter-
 esting features about a new location, and it
 will massively warp how we distinguish a
 Wasteland location from a Settlement one.
For example, a Wasteland locale with a farm
 at the center could be an eerie, upsetting
 sight as it lies barren and dead, whereas a
 farm within a lively settlement is a pleas-
 ant, welcome sight.
Once you've determined if a location is a
 Wasteland or a Settlement, roll a d20 to
 generate an [ICON]
Once you have,
 note the Icon in the Map Tab for this loca-
 tion and then generate a Location Truth.

LOCATION TRUTH
 Once an Icon is generated, it's time to
 generate a Truth about the location;
as
 mentioned earlier in this book, a Truth is
 a definable fact about the location you are
 creating, something that will affect every-
 one who travels or lives within it.
Depending on whether the location is a
 Wasteland or a Settlement, roll a d20 to:

■Settlement: [SETTLEMENT TRUTH]
 

■Wasteland: [WASTELAND TRUTH]
 

Once you have rolled to generate the Truth
 of this location, apply it to the Truths
 section of the location on your Map Tab.

Irradiated Locations

When generating a location Truth, there
 is a chance that you may end up with an
 Irradiated location. In Fallout, most of
 the Wasteland is irradiated in one way or
 another, but the Irradiated Truth here is
 more indicative of a location overburdened
 with Rads, like the Glowing Sea (Fallout 4)
 or the entrance to Vault 87 (Fallout 3).

When operating in an Irradiated location,
 your character is near constant danger,
 which can be represented however you
 choose, but we advise the following method.

When in an Irradiated location, follow these
 rules:

■When Traveling into or out of an
 Irradiated 
location, suffer 1 Radiation
 Damage.

■When starting or finishing an Encounter
 within an Irradiated location, suffer 1
 Radiation Damage.

■After completing any Safe Actions within
 an Irradiated location, suffer 1 Radiation
 Damage.

Items, Truths, or Perks that would allow your
 character to resist the effects of Radiation
 may allow your character to avoid this
 Damage;
such as taking a dose of Rad-X will
 allow you to avoid suffering the Damage for
 an action, or wearing a Hazmat Suit allow-
 ing you to avoid it entirely.
How you interact with Radiation is up to you,
 but we advise approaching it with the same
 mentality as you would in any other Fallout
 game.

FLESH IT OUT

Having a baseline of information about a
 location, as well as an Icon and Truth is a
 wonderful way of giving any space a strong
 description;
but the wealth of a story is
 in the details-so in this section, you will
 be able to add details to these locations,
 should you so choose.
You can, of course, add as much or as little
 as you would like, but the more you focus
 on bringing the world you're inhabiting
 through your character to life, the more
 rewarding you will find your time with
 Wasteland Wanderer.
Bringing out the details of a location can be
 as simple as asking yourself some questions
 about the area, and then answering those
 questions in your Journal or on the Map
 Tab.
These questions often fall under two
 core categories:

What do you see?
 What is left to see?
Both of these questions are relevant to both
 Settlements and Wasteland locations, but
 we will now look at how you can elaborate
 on them.
SETTLEMENTS

Settlements are often a welcome rest stop
 for exhausted travelers, but can also be
 dangerous compounds run by the most
 well-armed factions in the Wasteland.
How
 Settlements present and what they feel like
 are as varied as the people who live there.
Before you work out anything else, how-
 ever, you must first note your Settlement
 Reputation.
Settlement Reputation
 What people think of you isn't always as
 important as you may think, unless those
 people are heavily armed Wasteland
 Dwellers.
In Wasteland Wanderer, what a
 settlement thinks of your character is dictated
 by their Reputation within that Settlement.
Each Settlement has its own view on your
 character, which should be listed in the
 Settlement Truths section for each Settlement.
When you first visit a Settlement, roll a d20
 to generate [SETTLEMENT REPUTATION]
 
This represents the Settlement's initial
 view on your character and can be adapted
 as time goes forward by completing Side
 Quests for the people who call it home.
Building interesting Settlements is easy if
 you keep in mind the following questions:

What do you see?
When you first arrive, there are a number
 of things that you can answer about the
 Location in question.
Think about how
 these answers differ from other places you
 have been and what the underlying reason
 might be.
■ Are there any monuments visible from
 outside?

■What are the defenses like?

■How do people react to your arrival?
■ Are there any requirements to enter?
 ■ Is there anyone else arriving at the same
 time?
■What do the surroundings look like?

What is left to see?
You'll be able to discover more about the
 Settlement once you've been there a while.
Pick a few of these questions to answer
 about each Settlement you visit, and always
 feel free to add in your own questions.

■How was the Settlement founded?

■Who is in charge, and how do they rule?

■Who are the most important people?
■What do people eat?

■Why is this Settlement here?

■What interesting groups call this place
 home?
■What are the dangers of living here?

■Does this place have any interesting
 holidays?
■What are the key events in this

Settlement's history?

What do the residents think about this
 place?
■What do others think about this place?

■What resources does this Settlement

have access to?
■Are there any residents who are disliked
 by others?

■Are there any resident Ghouls or Robots?
Once you have answered a few of these
 questions, you should have a much stronger
 mental picture of what this Settlement
 looks like!
Do not feel like you need an

answer to every question above before you
 can finalise your Settlement and move onto
 your Encounter, as you can always return to
 flesh out more about the Settlement as time

progresses.
When you're finished asking
 these questions, you only have one more
 to answer before moving to the Encounter
 step of your Round-what is the
 Name of your Settlement?

ENCOUNTER

As much as Fallout is a story about the open
 Wasteland, it's also a story about those who
 call it home-the men, women, beasts,
 mutants, and other things stranger still that
 walk its expanse.
When people meet for
 the first time in the Wasteland, it is rarely
 a dull experience.
Often, bullets and blood
 are traded far more eagerly than words, but
 on occasion, caps and goods swap hands,
 and people part ways as something partly
 resembling friends.
Encounters are what bring a narrative step
 to your character's travels throughout the
 Wasteland, a randomized meeting or event
 that occurs once the character arrives at
 their destination.
Encounters are often
 chance meetings, poor timing, and luck
 bringing one party in contact with another;
other times, they are carefully planned
 interactions organized by whoever the
 character meets.
Either way, an encounter is
 a rich opportunity to expand your narrative
 in an interesting way.
CREATING AN ENCOUNTER

Generating an Encounter is a quick process
 intended to give you the groundwork to
 expand on and decide your own reasons for
 the Encounter occurring.
To generate an Encounter, do one of the
 following:

■If you are in a Settlement, roll a
 d20 to generate a [SETTLEMENT
 ENCOUNTER]

■If you are in the Wasteland, roll a d20 to
 generate a [WASTELAND ENCOUNTER]
 

Some results will ask you to generate NPCs,
 Items, or other things, whereas other
 results will only give a description of what
 you will encounter, but all will come with a
 single question.
This question is the jump-
 ing-off point for your storytelling, so once
 you've rolled up the Encounter, note it in
 the Journal and answer the question posed
 before you move to the next step and Take
 Action.
Blocker Encounters

Throughout your adventure, you will be
 aiming to complete your Main Quest, a
 goal that will require you to travel to Blocker
 Locations.
When you do so, you will need to
 check your Blocker Table and remind your-
 self of what your Blocker is.
Some Blockers
 will specifically tell you what to do when you
 arrive at the Location; others will not.
When
 they don't, use your initiative when generat-
 ing your Encounter to tie the two together;
or rather than rolling to generate, select the
 choices that best match the Blocker you're
 facing.

GENERATING FOES AND NPCS

A lot of Encounters begin with your charac-
 ter meeting someone-a Raider, trader, or,
 as in the above example, a musician.
When
 this happens, you will need to Generate
 an NPC or Foe.
Both are simple processes
 that will introduce a new (and potentially
 dangerous) face into your story.
Both character types require rolling on a
 number of generation tables, but where you
 record this information and how you con-
 tinue differs depending on the character
 type, which are listed in the appropriately
 named sections.
NPCS

When meeting an NPC, you should first
 decide whether you have met them before.
If you decide that the information already
 presented by the Encounter means it makes
 sense for you to know them, then take a
 look at your Data Tab and choose an appro-
 priate NPC to be here waiting for you.
Otherwise, you will need to generate a
 new NPC! To do so, you pick up 2d20 and
 [GENERATE NPC]
Unlike many Generation rolls, an NPC
 generation takes several rolls on a variety
 of tables to pull together who the charac-
 ter is.
It's recommended that, as you move
 through the tables, you record each roll in
 your Journal to make notes on who this
 character will be, which will start helping
 you determine their place in your story.
Once you have generated each of these, you
 will have created the NPC your character
 will be meeting, so all that remains is to
 add them to your Data Tab.
To do so, note
 down the NPC's name, Truth, and Location
 in one of the NPC slots available;
once this
 is completed, the NPC is finished!

Upon meeting a brand new NPC for the
 first time, your character Gains 1XP.
You may use this NPC throughout your
 adventure, adding more Truths to them
 each time you use the Meet Action
 to visit them.

FOES

Foes are regularly generated throughout
 Wasteland Wanderer, most commonly as
 part of an Encounter.
To generate a Foe,
 grab a d20 and [GENERATE FOE]
Unlike NPCs, Foes are generated in two
 steps: determining the Foe Type and then
 what the Foe (or Foes) are.
This is done
 by rolling on the Foe Type table first, and
 then rolling on the indicated type table to
 determine who your Foes are and what
 they're doing.
Once you have rolled on both, you have
 the basis of your Encounter and you can
 use your better judgement to determine
 the spacing of the Encounter-i.e., how far
 away you are from the foes, do they know
 you're there, etc. If neither the Encounter
 nor Foe description gave a clear enough
 description of the layout of the Encounter,
 remember you can generate a [COMBAT
 STATE]to determine this for you.
Once you have generated your Foes and are
 aware of the layout of the Encounter before
 moving ahead, you need only determine if
 you are in Danger.
Example:
 Creating a Foe Encounter
 In the last example, Nick Rose discovered
 there was a Musician living in the ruins of an
 old Red-Rocket garage-someone he had
 every intention of having a lovely chat with.
However, what would have happened if Ben
 had generated a Foe instead?
In this example, let's imagine Ben had rolled a
 6, a Foe Encounter instead.
This means he must
 now Generate a Foe, so he begins by deter-
 mining the Foe Type and rolls a 9, determining
 his Foes as Feral Ghouls.
Knowing this, he
 moves to the Feral Ghoul Generation
 Table, rolls a 13, and reads the result.
Now, instead of a potentially lovely conver-
 sation with a musician, Nick hears no music,
 only the low rasping breath of a Glowing
 One inside the building.
Closing toward it,
 he can see the creature is leaning in to stare
 at a cracked terminal screen, its face pressed
 against the broken glass.
The Ghoul has no
 idea he's here yet, as the description suggests
 its focus is elsewhere.
It also gives a clear
 idea that the Ghoul is likely inside, meaning
 that Ben doesn't need to generate a Combat
 State, as the setup of the Encounter is fairly
 straightforward.

DANGEROUS NPCS

Sometimes, over the course of your travels,
 your character may come across a hostile
 NPC, or even just an NPC on the road with
 enough reason to distrust your character
 that they could be considered a potential
 threat.
When this happens, you will need to
 create a Dangerous NPC.
Dangerous NPCs
 differ from Foes in that they often do not
 cleanly fit into any of the aforementioned

Foe Types, and are more likely to be charac-
 ters who may have a significant impact on
 your story.
In short, Dangerous NPCs 
 are potential crux points for your narrative,
 characters that may change the direction of
 your story in a way that a Foe may not.
When called to create a Dangerous NPC,
 follow all the normal steps to [GENERATE
 NPC] Once you have done so, you
 can use the [DANGEROUS NPC]
 tables to modify the character and generate
 them a Stat Block.
Once that has been completed, the NPC
 can still be interacted with as any other
 NPC;
however, should you ever alter your
 Reputation with that character (or their
 Faction) to Hostile, or take a hostile action
 against them you will become in Danger
 and they will be treated as any
 other Foe in regards to Actions and Combat.
Multiple Dangerous
 NPCA

In some Encounters, you may be asked to
 generate Dangerous NPCs, rather than
 create one from the natural progression of
 your story.
When this happens, you roll for
 [DANGEROUS NPC GROUP] which
 will generate the number of NPCs and what
 they're actively doing.
From there, create a single Stat Block using
 the [DANGEROUS NPC]to repre-
 sent each member.
If you wish, you may
 then [GENERATE NPC]for as many
 of the groups as you choose, though their
 Faction should match that generated during
 the Dangerous NPC Group process.
This
 will allow you to add as much, or as little
 characterization to the group as you wish,
 depending on how you want the Encounter
 to appear within your story.

TAKE ACTION

Taking Action is what gives us the onus
 in our stories, the ability to dictate how
 we act and therefore how we interact with
 situations beyond our control.
In this stage
 of the Round, you will direct your character
 to Take Action in the Encounter and then
 decide what they will be in the Location
 afterwards.
Once you have generated both your
 Encounter and any NPCs or Foes present
 within it, it's time to decide how your char-
 acter will interact with it.
However, how
 they will interact depends on whether the
 Encounter will put them in Danger
 or not.
In the Take Action stage, your character
 is looking to resolve the Encounter that
 you generated in the previous stage.
This
 is a mostly self-led experience, with you
 determining what Action your character
 takes and using the results of those Actions
 to weave the next stage of the story.
When an Encounter begins, you should
 have a clear Goal you are trying to achieve,
 like "Trade with the Trader', 'Survive the
 Raider ambush', or 'Make friends with the
 stranger'. Once you have achieved or failed
 at that goal through the use of Actions, you
 will know to finish the Encounter itself.

1. Determine Danger-In this Step, you
 determine if the Encounter is Dangerous
 at its onset.

2. Complete the Encounter: In this Step,
 you take Actions to try and Complete
 the Encounter.

3. Take Additional Actions: In this Step,
 after completing the Encounter, you
 make any additional Actions you would
 like to 
take in the Location.

4. Complete Stage: In this Step, you finish
 the Take Action Stage and prepare to
 Journal.

Take Action Example:
 Set Up

As covered in an earlier Example, Ben's
 Wasteland Wanderer character Nick Rose
 has come across a Glowing One in an aban-
 doned Red Rocket Gas Station; this forms the
 opening of his Encounter-with Nick outside
 of the building looking in to see the Glowing
 One inside.
From the setup, Ben determines
 that his main aim for this Encounter is to
 remove the Glowing One as a threat.
TAKE ACTION FLOW

The Take Action stage is the most freeform
 portion of a Round, where you aim to finish
 your Encounter, take any other Actions you
 wish to take in the location, and then finish
 the stage;
this is broken down into four
 steps:

DETERMINE DANGER

The Wasteland is an inherently dangerous
 place;
between the radiation, raiders, and
 radscorpions, it's difficult to find anywhere
 that could even remotely be considered
 "safe.
In Wasteland Wanderer, however, we
 use the term Danger, to represent when the
 decisions you are making could actively

cause your character to enter Combat, or
 otherwise cause them to suffer Damage.
To determine if your character is in Danger,
 ask the following questions:

■Did the Encounter rolled imply Danger
 or threat?
■Does the location I am in have a Truth
 that implies immediate Danger or
 threat?
■Does the location I am in have a Blocker
 or Quest that implies immediate Danger
 or Threat?
If the answer to all of these questions is
 'No', then your character is not consid-
 ered to be in Danger at the start of the
 Encounter and may therefore make Safe
 Actions.
However, if the answer
 to any of those questions is Yes, then the
 character is in active Danger, and may only
 make Dangerous Actions;
instead, they will
 only be able to take actions that will remove
 the threat from the situation or otherwise
 make the Encounter Safe.
Take Action Example:
 Determine Danger

Looking at the situation from the previous
 example, Ben determines that right now
 the Encounter is Safe as the only threat
 (the Glowing One) is inside a building and
 unaware of Nick, so he could perform any
 number of Safe actions he chooses to try and
 remove the threat.
However, he knows that going inside
 the building would make the situation
 Dangerous, and that any complications
 rolled during Safe Actions may lead to
 the Ghoul becoming aware and leaving
 the building, also making the situation
 Dangerous.
What is Dangerous?

The determination of being in Danger or
 not is mostly up to you as you play through
 Wasteland Wanderer, and it can often
 be a case of gut feelings over a hard and
 fast rule.
In short, being in Danger means
 that every time you make an Action, there is
 a chance of an immediate and potentially
 dangerous consequence.
For example, being locked in a room with
 a Feral Ghoul that doesn't know you're
 there is a Dangerous Encounter, because
 there is a chance with everything you do
 that the Ghoul may become aware of you
 and attack.
Whereas being outside of that
 room and able to look through a reinforced
 window to see that Ghoul would not be
 considered Dangerous as it cannot directly
 affect you.
Remember that how an Encounter starts
 may not be how it continues.
In the same
 example as above, the Encounter remains
 Safe until you elect to open the door to the
 room containing the Ghoul, at which point
 it becomes Dangerous.
Though if you were
 to then defeat that Ghoul, the situation once
 more becomes Safe.
COMPLETE THE ENCOUNTER

In this step, you make whatever Actions
 you feel will help you overcome and com-
 plete the Encounter that you've generated.
Depending on the Encounter itself, there
 could be any number of variances in how to
 achieve this, but regardless of the method
 itself, it will be undertaken by completing a
 set of Safe or Dangerous Actions.

SAFE AND DANGEROUS
 ACTIONS

When making Actions as part of an
 Encounter, your Pick a Path Action choices
 are split between Safe and Dangerous
 Actions, which dictate when they can be
 used.
■Safe Actions can be used without

putting yourself at immediate risk of
 taking Damage.
Mostly, these Actions are
 resolved around resting, exploring, inves-
 tigation, and meeting friendly NCPS.
■Dangerous Actions are used to try and
 get you out of a Dangerous Encounter
 and hold inherent risk of leading to
 Damage if failed.
You cannot use Safe Actions while in
 Danger, but you can use Dangerous Actions
 while Safe, though doing so will often
 make the Encounter Dangerous.
When in a
 Dangerous Encounter, it will only become
 Safe once you have taken Actions that
 would render it safe, either by removing
 whatever threat is present or leaving the
 Location.
Make Your Own Actions

As the Make Your Own Path Skill Tests
 are more freeform than the Pick a Path
 Actions, it's impossible in this book to deter-
 mine the variance of every possible interac-
 tion of Skill Tests;
therefore, it is advisable to
 use your best judgment when deciding if a
 Skill Test is Safe or Dangerous.
Safe Actions

■Approach: Put yourself into
 a Dangerous situation on purpose, or
 get the jump on foes who have yet to
 notice you, or turn a Safe situation into a
 Dangerous one.
■ Clear Main Quest Blocker:

 complete. Gain XP.
■ Complete Side Quest: Declare
 a Side Quest is complete. Gain Rewards
 and XP.
■Explore: Learn more about the
 Location you are in.

■ Find Supplies: Hunt for or pur-
 chase Supplies to keep you fed during
 your travels.
■Level Up: Spend XP to gain
 new Perks.

■Meet: Visit a known NPC to
 gain Quests, improve your Relationship,
 and catch up with the local news.
■Patch Up: Use Chems and your
 Medicine Skill to remove Injuries.
■Rest: Take some time to Heal
 and gather your wits.
■Mod: Take some time to Repair
 equipment and Mod items.
■Scavenge: Loot your surround-
 ings for Caps and Gear.
■Trade: Meet with a trader to
 spend your well-earned caps. Usually,

Dangerous Actions

■De-escalate: Try to reason with

■ Oppose: Try to use force to
 defeat your Foe.
■Outwit: Try to use cunning to
 defeat your Foe.
■Retreat: Try to escape the
 Location and end the Encounter.

JOURNAL

The final stage of a Round in Wasteland
 Wanderer is journaling what you've done
 during the Round-or, in other words,
 writing your story!
After encountering the inhabitants of the
 Wasteland, surviving its many horrors,
 and exploring a new part of the world, it is
 time for you to set pen to paper (or voice
 to dictaphone) and create a journal entry
 covering the time since your last entry (or,
 if this is your first entry, the time since you
 left your Vault).
Exactly how you choose to write your
 Wasteland Journal is down to personal pref-
 erence, and may even change as you play.
There's no right or wrong option-it's up
 to you. However, as a jumping off point, we
 have listed a few prompts below.

ADD A DATE

Most of your adventure is likely to take
 place in the 2280s, two centuries after the
 end of the old world.
It can be useful to
 pick a date and add it to the start of each
 journal entry (updating it by a day or so
 each time you travel).
This helps give a
 sense of time and can make a particularly
 long adventure feel like an epic.
There is no hard and fast rule on how long
 a period of time a Round represents, so use
 your best judgment and go with what feels
 right.
EPILOGUE

At some point, your adventure will come to
 an end, whether it is due to the completion
 of your Main Quest, succumbing to
 your Injuries, or simply deciding to
 settle down.
When this happens, it is time for you to
 write an Epilogue, a final journal entry that
 covers the last moments of your character's
 adventure.
There are many ways to do this,
 and none of them are wrong.
Some play-
 ers like to make one last recording of the
 moments leading up to their demise, while
 others prefer to take their previous journal
 entries and summarise their entire adven-
 ture in memory.
