# Fallout: Wasteland Wanderer - Chapter 2: The Wasteland of America

## Chapter 2

### THE WASTELAND OF AMERICA

In this chapter, we discuss the world of Fallout specifically the Wasteland of America, where your adventures will take place. This chapter is broken into three sections: The Wasteland, which details the Wasteland as a whole; The People, which details those who call it home; and The Story, which gives an overview of the themes of Fallout and how the world embodies them.

As a whole, this chapter will introduce you to the world of Fallout and act as a great jumping-off point to start building out your own story, giving you time to consider who you want to be in the Wasteland, helping guide your choices in Chapter 3: Character Creation (pg.50).

"If you want to see the fate of democracies, look out the windows." - Robert House

### THE WASTELAND

The America of 2077 was an idyllic place on the outside. Food was plentiful, the dawn of microfusion energy all but wiped out the nation's dependence on fossil fuels, and despite the very real nature of it, the Sino-American War was little more than a passing thought for the day-to-day American. Automation minimized the need for a blue-collar workforce, and the government worked hand-in-hand with mega-corporations to make sure that each day was better than the last. The rampant taxation, civilian abductions, shadow government, and blunt subjugation of Canada were nothing more than backward thoughts to ignore, red-spread propaganda to break apart the morals of true blue Americans. Or so the people were told, and few possessed the level of distrust or skepticism to do anything other than either accept the lies or wallow in them. So, say what you will about the Great War, but at least sudden atomization is honest. Brutally, horrifyingly honest. Nuclear fire is simple like that.

There was no death toll; no one was there to track it, and the few who could have had more pressing matters. They were huddled in masses underground, watching from afar in bomb-proof bunkers, sipping cocktails with disinterested indifference, or otherwise spared from being turned into little more than atomized dust upon the blast wind. Regardless of how, they survived. Humanity, in one form or another, continued. That was two centuries ago, and life, as it tends to do, has adapted. The old cities still stand, burnt-out corpses with barely the power to drive even the most vestigial aspects of their once-thriving urban sprawls. Mutants, survivors, and more nest within rotting alcoves and the remnants of the world before, clutching at the trinkets of yesteryear just so they can try and see tomorrow. Huge, scorched plains stretch out between places where once greenery, fields, and farms spread over the landscape, now just a cancerous skin - and stretching highways that once formed the veins of commerce, carrying goods from place to place, lie ruptured and open. Trucks full of items that one would have killed to own are now splattered with the blood of people who died just for a sip of water. Suffice it to say that America has changed, and the dream that founded it has mutated as much as its people.

Nature is no longer natural. Even the cockroaches and rats have had their genes twisted into new helixes better suited to survive. Vermin that you could have crushed underfoot are now more than happy to chew that foot off before making a meal of the rest of you. Flora is near unrecognizable, flowers and fruit often bulbous with tumorous growths that, according to the new wave of farmers and fleecers, do nothing more than enhance the 'natural' flavors or properties. Despite everything, capitalism is alive and well in the Wasteland, with good old Nuka-Cola bottle caps as the new coin of the realm, one of the few unifying factors across the new United Wastes of America. Another is that even smothered in the fallout, with enough background rads to make a Geiger counter tick like a radio, America perseveres.

#### THE WEST COAST
All of the Wasteland has been the stage for change, though few areas have been a hub for it as much as the western coast of 'America,' or as it is now known, New California. Post-war California has grown, become powerful, fallen, and been rebuilt more than many spaces of the Wasteland, with numerous seeds of the future planted within its mostly barren soils. As would be expected of a country bombed to hell and back, America underwent a severe climate shift from coast to coast, with huge swathes of once luscious farmland reduced to little more than a barren desert. The West Coast is an especially poignant example of this change—water shortages are commonplace in the extreme heat of the area, making well-supplied settlements and cities literal oases in the otherwise inhospitable climes. Befitting a desert, cacti are commonplace—still remarkably similar to the specimens you would expect to see pre-war, but other flora have been twisted into new strains, cultivated by a new wave of farmers to try and eke out a living from the land. In more settled spaces, though, you would often forget that you were living in any form of post-apocalypse, with luscious green fields providing plentiful harvests of vegetables, fuel, and, importantly, grazing. Fields in which one would regularly find Brahmin, the heavily mutated cattle of not just the West Coast—but much of the Wasteland. Though the Brahmin are domesticated, they are the rarity rather than the norm—with a menagerie of deadly wildlife filling the space between settlements, Deathclaws, Radscorpions, and the regionally specific breed of Geckos make the prospect of travel always worth a second thought, to even the most renowned Courier.

Despite the harsh climate and dangerously ravenous inhabitants, the West Coast has been the birthplace of many of the Wastelands' most influential factions. Both the Brotherhood of Steel and the Super Mutant race originated in the same place, Mariposa Military Base. The Hub standardized bottle caps, the expected currency among the more 'settled' areas of the Wasteland, even if the numerous Raider clans happily substitute those for a few bullets and some spilled blood. Vault 15 opened and began down the long journey that would lead to the foundation of the New California Republic (NCR) and the numerous armed conflicts they would engage in...for the betterment of the people, of course.

In a similar vein, as grand factions grow, so do stories of grand heroes and their foes. The West Coast knows no shortage of people whose names have become Wasteland legend. The Vault Dweller's quest to save their Vault and struggle against The Unity. The Chosen One's conflict with the Enclave. A lone Courier's quest for vengeance. Even a young woman hunting to find her father. Each person to walk across the Wasteland has the opportunity to shape it, but it seems those from the West Coast just have a knack for it.

##### THE UNITY
When the Super Mutants first started to march out from Mariposa Military Base, they did so under the banner of The Unity. The dream of one Richard Gray, The Unity was a vision of the future where humanity had been 'perfected' via mutation, where each body was a canvas, F.E.V. (Forced Evolutionary Virus) the paint, and The Master the artist. For years, The Unity operated under his rule, with Super Mutants significantly more advanced than the dull specimens that ravage the wastes now. Operating in secret, The Unity worked to create the next wave of dominant life for the world and was ruthless in the execution of their vision. If it wasn't for the intervention of a Vault Dweller on a simple quest for a water chip, who knows what could have happened?

##### THE BONEYARD
Once the 'City of Angels,' Los Angeles was one of many cities reduced to a skeleton by the Great War. The heart of the American cinema industry died the moment the bombs dropped, but thanks to nearby Vaults (both demonstratory and experimental), a handful of people survived. Those survivors went on to form gangs, found Adytum, and continue the regional tendency for resilience and innovation. In time, as the NCR grew in influence, The Boneyard was drawn into its structure, even holding power within the greater NCR Senate.

##### THE HUB
In the Wasteland, a lot of things are more valuable than life, bullets, caps, food, a (barely) mint Grognak #1, and water. Clean water is the lifeblood of the Wasteland; everyone wants it, everyone needs it, few have it and there certainly isn't enough to go around. In The Hub, though, water was readily available if you had the caps. A conglomerate of various merchant caravans built up around an 'oasis,' it began as a few pitched tents, with goods and services offered and bartered—but time progressed and enough caps flowed through that those tents rapidly grew into one of the most successful trading posts in the Wasteland. Of all the caravans that helped grow The Hub into what it is today, few are as noteworthy as the Water Merchants, whose trade has ensured the health and well-being of those with enough caps to pay their bills. Now, The Hub has joined with the NCR, and its visible prosperity is a sterling example of what can be achieved by those who work long and hard enough. The Hub is also incredibly adept at hiding the worst of itself—the inequality of its people and the painfully wide economic variance between those on top and those crushed underfoot.

##### SHADY SANDS
From a small settlement to the capital of a republic, Shady Sands was the birthplace of the NCR. Beginning as a small farm sprawling out from Vault 15, Shady Sands was given the best start any Wasteland settlement could ask for by making use of a G.E.C.K. (Garden of Eden Creation Kit) to turn the soil into something actually capable of bearing life. From there, exponential growth led to an evolution from settlement to city, with a rare but fully functioning infrastructure. Compared to the rest of the Wasteland, Shady Sands was most akin to a pre-war city, offering all the safety and modern conveniences one would expect.

##### MOJAVE WASTELAND
When most of America was blown into the apocalypse, the Mojave was mostly spared. There is a curious irony in the fact that while most of the civilized world was bombed into a wasteland, the Mojave was already a dust bowl. The few bombs that did land did little more than alter the wildlife. The few targets of any value, for those missiles looking for them, were either too sparse to worry about or too well-defended to even reach. Vegas was one such target. No small number of warheads were pointed at Sin City, and of that number, none managed to hit their intended location. Most were picked out of the sky by the various defense systems implemented by the head of Rob-Co industries, Mr House—who had more than a vested interest in the city's survival. The few missiles that weren't destroyed, disabled, or otherwise rendered little more than hunks of flying metal landed in the surrounding area still ensuring devastation for most of the poor souls who had nowhere to hunker away from the impending doom, but leaving most of the Mojave unspoiled.

Of course, humanity still hid away for a time, dealing with whatever new hell the rampant radiation had brought. Mutants, starvation, droughts, all of those classic post-nuclear-war problems. As the isolated communities of the Mojave dealt with their issues, the neighboring states did the same, though as powers further afield grew, they ignored the Mojave for the most part. Centuries passed, tribes germinated in the dust and sands, even claiming back some of the old casinos and forming themselves into reflections of the past, though with a certain Wasteland flair. It wasn't until the NCR kicked down the door at Hoover Dam that the Mojave really sprung to life, and the lights of Vegas—New Vegas—blared back into life.

Mr. House had, by unknown means, survived and negotiated with the heads of the NCR to keep their military stranglehold to a minimum and maintain New Vegas's independence. Those negotiations gave way to the Treaty of New Vegas and started the 'peaceful' coexistence of the various groups who stayed within the Mojave. The NCR was happy sending power back home from Hoover Dam, Mr. House worked within the confines of New Vegas on his own aims, and everyone else was left more or less to their own devices. Until, of course, Caesar's Legion crossed the state boundary, with their eyes firmly locked onto both The Strip and Hoover Dam. With no shortage of either warm bodies or bloody intent, the Legion announced itself violently, launching a doomed assault on Hoover Dam before pulling back to their own freshly carved slice of the Mojave. Which is where they've waited ever since, carefully probing the NCR's defenses, looking for weaknesses, as the NCR does the exact same in return. Both sides wait for either to make a mistake as Mr. House watches from a distance, knowing that should anyone slip, he'll have every opportunity to prove that once again—the house always wins.

##### NEW VEGAS
Before the NCR's arrival, Vegas was little more than a playground for the Raider tribes lurking in the rubble of a city lost to disuse and a lack of maintenance. Once the New California Republic made itself known, revealing that civilization still existed in the world, a swarm of Securitrons rolled out at the behest of Mr. House. They summarily dragged the warring tribes into line, more a hostile takeover than anything else. Since then, power has been restored to the city, old tribes have become the Three Families of New Vegas, and Sin City has returned to the ways of old. Alcohol, prostitution, chems, caps, and chips are the lifeblood of the Strip, and Mr. House keeps a quiet hand pushing people in the right direction, his direction. Of all the things one expects in the modern Wasteland, tourists aren't one of them, but The Strip has no shortage of visitors willing to risk all they have on the road just to risk it all again at one of the casinos. However, they often find that whatever game they play, it was rigged from the start.

##### CAMP MCCARRAN
The NCR holds two main garrisons in the Mojave, Hoover Dam, and Camp McCarran, with the latter being their primary headquarters. Previously McCarran International Airport, the structures of the area survived the winds of time better than most. Alongside the monorail system connected directly to The Strip, there are few spots that are easier reinforced and resupplied than Camp McCarran. Though Hoover Dam is a superior tactical asset, most of the day-to-day running of the Mojave arm of the NCR passes through McCarran, acting as the nerve center of their military campaign against Caesar's Legion.

##### THE FORT
Otherwise known as 'Fortification Hill,' The Fort forms the current main camp of Caesar's Legion. From here, Caesar himself dictates each move of the Legion and houses both his own Praetorian guard and the numerous slaves under his command. As far as he knows, it is the locus of his power in the Mojave, one of many locations destined to fall to his will. However, this is not wholly the case, as an easily ignored weather monitoring station just outside of his fortifications contains a legion of Securitrons built centuries prior by Mr. House. While Caesar forces slaves to fight to the death for his amusement, below ground, a system of robotic mechanisms wait to spring to life and forge an army of gleaming metal at a moment's notice. All it would take is the right person, with the right key, to hand the Fort to a completely different owner.

##### JACOBSTOWN
Outside of the triple-faced power struggle of the Mojave, Jacobstown rests upon Mount Charleston in an old resort, though the clientele are far different from what would have been expected Pre-war. A rare sight for most, Jacobstown is a refuge for Super Mutants, those looking for a life apart from the violence often (and fairly) attributed to their kind, under the leadership of a particularly aged Super Mutant, Marcus. Chiefly, Jacobstown acts as a home for the Nightkin, a breed of Super Mutant boasting a higher-than-average intelligence but suffering from severe mental degradation. Marcus is taking measures to try and protect his ailing kin while searching for a way to help them. All the while trying to ensure Jacobstown's prosperity and keep it from attracting the wrong kind of attention.

#### THE CAPITAL WASTELAND
Unsurprisingly, the Capital of the United States was one of, if not the most devastated section of the country when the Great War hit. With wave after wave of bombs causing untold devastation, rippling fires engulfed the city, monuments were toppled, and unaware civilians were torn apart by blast winds. When the heart of the USA died, it did so spectacularly. Despite this, the government had been preparing for such an eventuality, reinforcing buildings, preparing back-up plans, and otherwise bracing for the impact to come. However, all the preparation in the world wouldn't have allowed them to weather the storm, and though the survival rate was higher in the Capital than in most places, the city to which the survivors returned was not a hospitable one. Fires and radiation had rendered most of the Capital uninhabitable, and any efforts to keep control over survivors became more difficult each day as desperation and panic settled into those few who had just watched their entire world washed away by glowing flames. Many abandoned the city, fleeing to what they hoped were greener pastures. A handful stayed and tried desperately to make something, anything, work. Settlements were forged out of nothing more than that which could be scavenged in locations far from ideal, but perseverance was a requirement. But each time a hopeful situation appeared, the Capital was quick to oppose it with some new horror. Raiders were more common than settlers, and mutated creatures were always eager for unaware survivors to be rapidly repurposed into lunch. To say nothing of the brutal strain of Super Mutants that seemed to be tearing out of the ground itself to wage war on anything that wasn't them.

No matter what, it seemed that the Capital Wasteland was lost to bloodshed; violence had become the only real method of communication left to most. Even when the Brotherhood of Steel arrived, D.C. pushed back. As part of the Brotherhood's never-ending campaign to obtain advanced technology, they came with an objective in mind and rapidly found that objective changing to try and combat the Super Mutant threat building in the Capital's heart. And for every moment they spent battling mutants, Raiders and Slavers ran free. If they turned their attention to the human threat, the Super Mutants doubled in both aggression and numbers. In every direction was a danger, and there was never enough time, bullets, or bodies to adequately deal with any of them. So they took one of the few options left to them: they consolidated their power into one location and planned for their next step. And in that moment, another group arose, eager to fill the space abandoned by the Brotherhood and use it to their advantage. The Enclave had returned with a new figurehead emboldened with the same purpose as the last time they had stood against the Wasteland. Genetic genocide. They sought to wipe the Capital clean again, to eliminate any trace of mutant life, no matter how minor the divergence. Now, only time will tell if the Capital will remain as it is or be purified.

##### MEGATON
When all hope is lost, people look for it wherever they can. And to begin with, that's what Megaton was—a hope. Looking for and denied shelter from Vault 101, settlers took refuge in a nearby crater from dust storms. Time passed, and that crater grew into a trade hub, which in turn grew into a town. To protect itself from creatures, Raiders, and whatever else may come to threaten them, walls were erected, and most dangers were separated from the growing population. Though, curiously, one of the greatest dangers to the towns is nestled at its very center—even an object of worship to some—an unexploded nuclear bomb. Whereas those in the town see it as little more than a curiosity to show off to travelers, others are more interested in the weapon's original intent.

##### RIVET CITY
Sometimes, remnants of the old world offer up a bounty. Rivet City is one such bounty, one of the most secure locations in the Capital, and a source of clean food for those willing to trade with them. Founded by a member of the now-defunct Naval Research Institute, Rivet City started as a research station and rapidly evolved into a full-fledged settlement as the leadership expanded and allowed outsiders within their heavily armored bulkhead. With a hydroponic farm, trading floor, housing, and more, Rivet City is the closest thing to the new heart of D.C. Though it isn't without its issues—like the Mirelurk infestation in the bow and regular attacks from Super Mutants and Raiders, to say nothing of internal politics. But if you consider the alternative, Rivet City may as well be paradise.

##### PROJECT PURITY
Built within the Jefferson Memorial is one of the best hopes for a new dawn in the Capital: Project Purity. Devised by a brilliant team of scientists, the project aims to purify all water within the Potomac River—an action that would bring a new wave of life to the Capital Wasteland. In every way, the actions of those attempting to finish the work are wholly good, but as is often said, no good deed goes unpunished. As eager as certain powers are to ensure safe, clean water is returned to the people, others wish to leverage the power offered by the project or even use it to completely reverse its intent and bring death, en masse, to the greater Wasteland. Regardless of who presses the button, the activation of Project Purity will send ripples in every direction.

##### PARADISE FALLS
Malls, should they have survived into the modern Wasteland, would be interesting affairs. Sadly, Paradise Falls proves that. Built from the skeleton of a shopping center lies a haven for both Raiders and Slavers. Paradise Falls is a marketplace for anything with a pulse, well known across the Capital Wasteland for the common 'shopping trips' undertaken by its inhabitants. Anyone can buy anyone here—with explosive slave collars acting as the best version of 'buyers' protection possible. Those with a moral compass pointing anywhere but south seem to agree that no one would shed a tear should Paradise Falls be wiped off the map, but with just enough guns and a readily deployed wall of unwilling flesh to protect them, the Slavers know that they're just not worth the trouble. Which is how they like it.

#### THE COMMONWEALTH
Boston, Massachusetts, one of the industrial workhorses of modern-day America—a state that dedicated every asset it could to the Sino-American war effort. Though not for patriotism, no, a far simpler methodology drove Boston: capitalism, the pursuit of profit. Few had the moral compunction to consider the human cost to their bottom line. Workers were treated as little more than commodities, to be exploited as one would exploit any other easily replaceable asset. The cost of living was driven so high that most were forced to work for days on end or rise to positions of power within businesses already so corrupt that it was impossible to reach any heights without sullying their spirit to do so. Not that the brightly colored signs, logos, and advertisements liberally littering the streets of Boston would allude to that. There are always appearances to keep, and the Commonwealth has developed a real knack for hiding its nasty little secrets just below the surface. However, the inequality regularly boiled to the top; riots in the streets over food shortages, automation taking jobs from Average Joe Americans, and more were commonplace and were met with all the kindness the normal citizen could expect from their loving government. Innocent blood filled the drains as American bullets put down American citizens in the name of America. Even before the bombs fell, people were fighting over food in Boston. So when the Great War came, it was little more than a pause from one legacy of violence directly into another.

Of the few mercies gifted to the Wasteland, Boston itself was spared any direct hits from the war, leaving the city standing and most buildings only at risk of disrepair and time. Not all of the Commonwealth was so lucky, with a singular explosion rocking the southwestern area of the map, blowing Sentinel Site Prescott and the surrounding landscape into a new existence as a glowing hellscape with radiation levels so absurdly high that it would remain deadly even centuries later. As is common following the Great War, mutation and transformation are rife in the Commonwealth, but the most curious change is that of the people. Prior to Armageddon, greed was the driving force in Boston, but forced to the edge, more people have banded together to look after one another than was ever the case beforehand. Old calls have gone out by the once forgotten Minutemen, underground cells move to save people built for servitude, and even the 'criminal-scum' of the world band together to try and just be good neighbors to one another. Not to suggest that the blood stains on the roads are any less rife. Various factions vie for control over the area, and people are still more than willing to murder one another for a bite to eat or a sip to drink. In that way, Boston hasn't changed much, and people still pass through for one reason or another. Even if it's just for a mirelurk roll and a look at the 'Great Green Jewel,' some say that a trip to the Commonwealth is life-changing—you could leave a completely different person from when you arrived.

##### DIAMOND CITY
As Boston was spared a direct nuclear attack, most of the city's structures were able to survive the worst of the blast waves. Among these were Fenway Park, which was poised to host the Baseball World Series mere hours after the bombs hit. Thanks to the robust walls, those inside the stadium survived and would go on to found Diamond City. Over time, the 'Great Green Jewel' would develop into one of the key settlements in the Commonwealth, weathering more than simple nuclear war and remaining strong throughout. Super Mutant hordes, cults, and their monsters, and more Raiders than you can count have broken against The Wall, and it seems unlikely that any threat outside the stadium will ever break their winning streak. Not all threats are so obvious, though, and rumors have begun to circulate that maybe the leadership of Diamond City is far less human than his anti-ghoul sentiments would lead some to believe. But those are just rumors—why trust those?

##### GOODNEIGHBOR
While some question the humanity of the Mayor of Diamond City, no one questions the humanity of the Mayor of Goodneighbor because he'll be the first to laugh about it. Goodneighbor is, above all, a place where neighbor looks after neighbor and no vice is turned down. Sex, chems, and rock and roll are core to the identity of the town; it's the place you go when you've got something to find or something to forget. John Hancock, the Mayor, is a firm believer in personal freedoms and will happily fight for people to live however they want to live, which is what Goodneighbor is all about. And while many have mistaken the town for a Raider haven, the truth couldn't be further from the truth as, despite appearances, the feeling of community here rivals even that of those in Diamond City.

##### THE GLOWING SEA
The main nuclear detonation in the Commonwealth wiped most of it from the map, replacing it with the Glowing Sea. Miles of rad-ruined landscape stretch as far as the eye can see. Radiation storms ripple across the sky, and blackened clouds blot out the sun as nuclear fallout still chokes the air. To the common man, there is no space in Massachusetts more antithetical to life, but to the teeming hordes of Ghouls, Deathclaws, and other mutants, the sea is a perfect home, with plenty of hunting and nesting grounds and even the occasional overly confident scavenger to play with. Even though the surface belongs to the mutant, below lies uncounted riches to those brave enough to hunt for them—when the area was brutalized by nuclear weapons, it buried dozens, if not hundreds, of buildings and hid all of those potential riches beneath a crust of nuclear ash.

##### NUKA-WORLD
A short ride from Boston lies the most Nuka'Riffic place on earth, Nuka-World! A sprawling theme park built in the image of the pre-war Nuka-Cola Company, it boasted six separate wonderful locations for the people of America to walk and forget their worries in a sugar-induced haze. Since the Great War, the park has seen a few changes and certainly can't claim to meet minimum safety standards anymore. Raiders have moved in, the park is now theirs to play with, and the cost of entry is a lot higher than it used to be.

#### APPALACHIA
There are few spaces in America that felt the sting of automation like Appalachia. While Boston struggled to keep up with the speed of industrialization, and the few who could find work were in a constant battle to prove their superiority to the machines being built to replace them, in Appalachia, they weren't even given the chance. A region defined by the mining industry saw the back-breaking labor of extraction transferred to auto-miners, forcing families that had worked with pickaxes and stone for generations into poverty. The battle of man versus machine formed the center of Appalachian politics for the days leading toward the Great War; riots were constant, and people were being driven from their homes. Even as the bombs fell, people were screaming for their rights until the air was ripped from their lungs and their bodies torn into atomic dust. In the days that followed, Appalachia was quick to recover from the Great War, at least in a way. The state was split into six distinct regions, with new settlers trying to make a home out of the harsh spaces and new threats ready to dissuade them.

##### ASH HEAP
All of Appalachia is inhospitable, but in Ash Heap, even the air is out to kill you. Previously the heart of the state's coal mining industry, much of the Heap is dominated by vast machinery and deep mines—the air is gripped tight by thick clouds of radiation-laden ash, and mines still spew out gouts of smoke and toxins. There are few sane people in Ash Heap, with only a few scarce Raiders of the Blood Eagle clan laying claim to old mining facilities, scavenging the technology-rich environment for whatever they can find. Where humanity falters, mutants thrive, with new and dangerous breeds resting in the crevices of the landscape. When one chooses to brave Ash Heap, they do so with a full pack, a Gas Mask, and a plan to get in and out as quickly as possible.

##### CRANBERRY BOG
So called due to the iconic red hue of its local flora, the Cranberry Bog is a treat for the eyes if nothing else. Rolling hills of bright red plants surround the future-city of Watoga, where machines had all but the run of the town. The human element in governance had been reduced to a single position—not that it helped when a weaponized computer virus struck just before the Great War. Now, the Bog is rife with rogue robots acting on fractured programming, as well as hordes of the Scorched that swarm under the shadows of beating Scorchbeast wings. At one time, the Brotherhood of Steel took root in the region, hoping to fight back against the plague, but sadly, it fell before their efforts could amount to anything beyond a few lost holotapes.

##### THE FOREST
Mostly spared the wrath of atomic war, The Forest remains similar to its pre-war visage, more overgrown perhaps and certainly with more aggressive critters nestling in the shadows of its trees. Running along the Ohio River, The Forest hides Vault 76 within its borders and the Vault Dwellers that emerge from its confines don't often take long to meet The Forest's other residents, the Cult of the Mothman.

##### THE MIRE
A spreading swamp laden with thick vines and gently throbbing plants, The Mire more than earns its name. Born from the detonation of a G.E.C.K., the Mire is a dense place to penetrate, with thick mists obscuring the area even when deep inside its heart. Strange monsters, commonly referred to as Cryptids, are often hunted here by those brave enough to do so, convinced the spoils outweigh the dangers. Whether that's true or not is as difficult to see as anything else in The Mire.

##### SAVAGE DIVIDE
Great mountains tear their way through the center of Appalachia, carving through the region and efficiently blocking the East from the West. This region, known as the Savage Divide, has become home to Raiders, Super Mutants, and Mole Miners in equal measure—making any hike through the ranges a dangerous endeavor. One of the greatest issues caused by the Divide is how difficult it makes communication between the factions looking to better Appalachia, delaying any aid they may offer one another in times of dire need.

##### SKYLINE VALLEY
A yet unexplored area of Appalachia, Skyline Valley is perpetually trapped beneath the roiling winds of The Storm, a glowing red tempest that pulses out from a seemingly abandoned manor house deep within the region. Stories have begun to pass from mouth to ear about the region already, with tales of Vault suit-clad Ghouls, their eyes and mouths hollow, leaking with electrical barbs, becoming a new and horrifying campfire tale.

##### TOXIC VALLEY
Grafton Steel Mill sits in the very center of the Toxic Valley, so named for the pungent waters that inexorably ooze through pallid, diseased Wasteland. Mutated creatures of all kinds prowl the river shores in search of prey or any form of nutrition that the water simply cannot offer. Telling how rich the soil may have once been, farms are common within the Toxic Valley—once bounteous fields now barren and gray, the rotten shards of corn stalks brushing in the breeze against new, twisted flora that survives on what little the valley can spare.

### THE PEOPLE

As before the Great War, America is but a bit part in the play of the Wasteland—the people that call it home are the true leads of the new stage, each more than willing to play their part. Innumerable settlements, factions, wanderers, and the like live their lives within the rad-soaked wilds of the Wasteland—forming societies and mirroring the birth of pre-war America with a new wave of frontiersmen and pioneers. It started slowly, of course, with the addled shadows of those lucky few who survived the bombs falling upon the now warped landscape. They took up what tools they could, tried to rebuild a foundation from which they may be able to help others. All the while, the others in question decided upon more violent means of survival—taking whatever they needed, no matter the cost. Even after near-total extermination, it seemed that humanity could find something to fight over just as violently as before. But, in a rare case of one-upmanship, from the ruins and experiments of the past came a new breed of creatures even more 'unnaturally' gifted in the ways of bloodshed than the humans whose rampant hatred led to the means of their creation. As is commonly said, hatred only leads to more hatred, and for the myriad mutants that came, that could not be more true. Humans hated the mutants for their existence, mutants hated humans for trying to punish their existence, more wars, more blood—more of the same. At least to start with.

In the 'modern' Wasteland, mutant and human life exists in a strained equilibrium—with each side occasionally making moves to unbalance that existence. Human settlements dot the landscape in every direction, from ten or so people taking what cover they can in once lavish homes to hundreds gathered in new 'cities' built up out of whatever was to hand. Within their hastily erected walls, mankind often rubs shoulders with ghoulkind—an offshoot of humanity that has been severely mutated by radiation. While it isn't uncommon to find a ghoul in a mostly human settlement, they are often discriminated against, persecuted, or sometimes even killed on sight—many ghouls elect to stay with others of their own kind. Some must even fear their ghoulish neighbors, as many have gone feral in the passing centuries—devolving to little more than raving 'mad things' with a fearsome desire for violence and blood and not a lick of their own minds to temper those needs.

Outside of human settlements, Raiders roam the Wasteland, bands of those who have long abandoned the idea of making anything new just taking whatever they need—be that via coercion, intimidation, theft, or just good old murder. Then there are the plethora of Wasteland mutants that have descended, or genetically ascended, from the species that came before them. Radscorpions with stingers sharp enough and long enough to pierce Power Armor. Radroaches that have grown to the size of a dog. Great lizard killing machines known far and wide for their unmatched lethality, the Deathclaws. And these only form the smallest part of the menagerie of wildlife, speaking nothing of the more intelligent mutated threats that stalk the pockmarked skin of the Wasteland, the Super Mutants, Cryptids, or others. The Wasteland is, if nothing else, a deeply varied locale; It's just a pity that most who call it home would kill you without a second thought.

### FACTIONS

The Wasteland is as much defined by those who call it home as the numerous dangers that make it difficult to be a home. Various Factions have appeared, made their impact—however minor it may be and vanished once again into the radioactive dust storms of the Wasteland. Over the next few pages, you will find information on some of the largest, most successful, and most well-known Factions you may encounter throughout your journey, as well as a series of small Plot Hooks to use as a jumping-off point for starting your own adventure!

#### BROTHERHOOD OF STEEL
"Ad Victoriam" or "To Victory" is but one of many sayings that pass the lips of those who call themselves members of the Brotherhood of Steel. 'Founded' June 20th, 2082, the Brotherhood was, and in many ways still is, intended to be a succession to what America was, what it represented, and more than anything, a warning of what it could become again. Originally led by Captain Roger Maxson, the 'Brotherhood' as it would come to be called was formed out of an act of brazen defiance against science gone mad. Treasonous in many eyes, Maxson acted against the actions of the United States Military and the inhuman experiments they carried out in the name of freedom. Whatever actions bore the Brotherhood have been lost in the rad-ridden years that have passed. The intentions Captain Maxson had as he ferried survivors to the Lost Hills bunker from Mariposa have been warped as easily as the DNA of a mutant and been reborn as something new—something aggrandized, self-assured. Something violent.

To the Brotherhood of the now, technology in all its forms is akin to relics, mythological items to be hoarded and protected, not for the sake of preservation—but simply to keep them from the hands of those who may use them. And should anyone be forced to carry the burden of wielding such tools, then it is only fitting that the steady, Power-Armored hands of the Brotherhood do so. And woe betide anyone or anything that stands before or against those morals. This has not always been the case. Through the years, the Brotherhood has taken many forms, an ironic evolution of intent that is belied by their current dogmatic disposition. In Appalachia and the Commonwealth, the Brotherhood worked with, if not for, the people of the Wasteland, protecting and arming them against the worst that the new world could offer. In Boston, they imposed themselves on the inhabitants, arriving upon wings of steel with the intent of burning away every mutant, monster, and scientific malfeasance they could find. And while their new leadership, Arthur Maxson, sought to eliminate the quasi-religious fervor that had fostered itself within the Brotherhood, the monastic formations of the order had already seeped into its being, becoming as much a part of the Brotherhood's identity as the Power Armor they bedeck themselves in.

The sickening irony of the Brotherhood is a simple one: at its inception, the Brotherhood was a force for positivity to protect humanity from the long-reaching shadows of the past. But now, with its new profundity for statecraft via enforcement, one of the last shadows the people of the Wasteland fear is that of a Brotherhood zeppelin overhead.

##### Plot Hooks: Brotherhood of Steel
* Brotherhood Aspirants are combing local settlements for potential recruits.
* A trader is on the run from the Brotherhood, hiding a piece of pre-war tech from them.
* A Brotherhood detachment has arrived to wipe out a particularly resilient Super Mutant tribe.
* You find an abandoned set of Brotherhood marked T-60 Power Armor; where is its owner?

#### CHILDREN OF ATOM
Faith is a powerful thing. It can drive people to acts previously thought impossible, to believe the incomprehensible, and accept the inhospitable. Few people of faith demonstrate this better than the Children of Atom. Followers of the godly personification of both nuclear destruction and division, the Children may not believe they live in an Eden, but they see the works of their god across every inch of the Wasteland, and they are quick to remind themselves and others of that fact. The Children have spread as readily as the fallout they idolize, though no one can identify their 'ground-zero' so to speak—and while their beliefs always follow the same core principles, often their method of reverence or the more esoteric factors of faith differ. But, at its center, the faith of Atom is built upon the precepts of one factor: each atomic body in existence bears within it another potential universe, a place where infinite lives are actualized and active. So, each being will, when divided by Atoms' gift (or, more specifically, any demise tied directly to nuclear radiation), cascade apart into untold universes, a blossoming of new life from the ending of just one. So, is death by division not just the allowance of new life? The elevation of the one into the many, the ultimate gift made possible by the touch of Atom? Such is the belief of the Children of Atom. However, it is easy for outsiders to hear this gospel and give into fear, believing that "those Atomites just want to blow everyone up," which may not be strictly true, though many variants of the Cult may indeed wish for such a world.

In truth, many Children only wish to divide themselves to live in relative peace until such a time that the 'Glow' spreads their atoms apart from living in irradiated locations, consumption of 'holy water,' or by more bombastic means. They do, of course, wish to see the 'Glow' spread across the Wasteland so that Atom's gift may touch more and will encourage it where they can with some of the more zealous Children even wielding weapons designed to cast great bursts of gamma radiation against their foes. Different offshoots of the Cult take very different stances, and each is a reflection of their leader. Where one may adopt a position of peaceful coexistence with those they call neighbors, another may (and did) declare war on the entire Commonwealth in the name of their god. In a state of curious irony, regardless of their beliefs, the various cults of Atom are innately divided. However, one must ask the question: if Atom requires more from its followers, what more can be offered to it than the great prayer of 2077 when Atom drew the entire world into its burning embrace?

##### Plot Hooks: Children of Atom
* A man with visions of a glowing city approaches you with whispers of a map.
* A group of Zealots have taken control of a nuclear silo and are feverishly searching for launch codes.
* A settlement has persecuted the otherwise peaceful commune nearby, threatening them with violence.
* A thick, radiation-laden mist is spreading from an old church with no end in sight.

#### MINUTEMEN
Some things can survive even Armageddon: Fancy Lad Snack Cakes, cockroaches, and, of course, ideals. An ideal, if spoken of, recorded, or otherwise passed on, can survive things no human or irradiated mutant could dream of. It is that thought, that immutable fact, that saw the Minutemen return to America when it needed them most and aided in their survival through all that came to push against them. Firstly, an idea. Secondly, a flag flying proudly from the walls of Diamond City, and then a name on the lips of Wastelanders all across the Commonwealth, The Minutemen promised to resurrect the words of their original namesake, "To protect the people at a minute's notice," and for a time, that is exactly what they did. Operating from Fort Independence, commonly known as 'The Castle,' the Minutemen spread across the Commonwealth, offering aid where they could, supporting the ill-fated foundation of the Commonwealth Provisional Government, and being the one flag that no settler feared. They moved under the command of a single person, their General, but with squads organized by Colonels. This allowed them to act from multiple locations with just enough autonomy to honor their promise and arrive where and when they were needed. Armed with their homemade laser muskets, as well as a bevy of other weapons and some good-old-fashioned American spirit, they helped the Commonwealth for well over 50 years. But nothing can last forever.

Their downfall came slowly but began with the inexorable approach of a Mirelurk Queen and her brood upon the shores of The Castle. And, notwithstanding the fierce defense of their home, the Minutemen simply could not hold off the fury of the oceanic assault, abandoning the Fort and, with it, their traditional command structure. Over the next few decades, those who remained operated less like an army and more akin to bands of similarly intended soldiers, with the rivalries and squabbles one would expect of such an organization. Their unity of purpose gone, the clock was ticking on the remaining Minutemen, and finally ran down at the town of Quincy. Tired of the failings of his old cause, a veteran by the name of Clint turned on his colleagues, joining up with the mercenary faction, The Gunners. He declared his betrayal suddenly at the town of Quincy, where he laid waste to not only the settlers but to the last remnants of the Minutemen who had been protecting them—to those he had called friends, who had seen his arrival as a sign of rescue, not execution. Now, a handful of survivors flee back into the Wasteland, led by the only Minuteman left, who is armed with little more than the clothes on his back, a laser musket, and, of course, an ideal.

##### Plot Hooks: Minutemen
* There were more survivors from Quincy, though the Gunners are making sport of them.
* Even if the Castle is lost, other Minutemen outposts still stand—keep it that way.
* Even with diminished numbers, the call for help still goes out; someone needs to keep the flag flying.
* At their height, Minutemen regularly cruised/patrolled the Wasteland. What if they still did?

#### RAIDERS
In this beautiful new world, there tend to be two types of people. Those who build and those who take. When some Wastelanders come together to form a new community, somewhere they feel safe to try and cultivate something resembling normality or even a home, it's almost guaranteed that in time, someone will come and try and take it from them. And when they do, the same fearful cry often rings out: "Raiders!" Coming in every shape and size imaginable, Raiders are a varied bunch—from the chem-addled hopeless to bloodthirsty maniacs, anyone can pull the moniker of Raider onto themselves like a filthy sack hood and begin to take whatever they choose from the Wasteland. Though they are often arrayed into 'clans,' Raiders tend to be any group of people who band together and use excessive violence to solve most, if not all, of their problems. Where a Wastelander would spend days, if not weeks, combing through ruins to find food and scrap to sell, a Raider will simply wait for some stupid sap to do all the hard work and then slit their throat, gun them down, or otherwise relieve them of their 'burden.'

Most Raider groups are small, numbering no more than two dozen members at most. Their way of life naturally lends itself to an inherently self-destructive end, often with one or two of their number killing the others and starting again with a new band elsewhere. However, some Raiders are either charismatic enough or, frankly, scary enough to form larger, longer-lasting groups, ones that go on to become Wasteland legends, a story told to keep wary settlers on their toes. The Great Khans of the Mojave. The Pack, Disciples, and Operators. Sudden-Death Overtime. Jackals. Fiends. All are names readily recognized by those who dare to live anywhere near the blood-dappled courts of these wannabe Wasteland Overlords, all despised and feared in equal measure. And despite the numerous attempts of mercenaries, sheriffs, wanderers, and others, it seems to be that every single time you wipe out one band, three more appear from nowhere, eager and armed to take over the space lost by their predecessors.

One thing that both unites and divides each separate clan, though, is their flair for the dramatic, which is almost rivaled by their sheer brutality. Each group tends to style themselves according to some esoteric desire or another, "clean" pressed suits with armored decoration, spike-smothered cage plates, or even simply a beaten-up old teddy bear head—no one can claim the Raiders don't have a semblance of personal style. So yes, while they may gun you down for your gear, for bad-mouthing them, or just for something to do, you can at least rest in the knowledge they'll show your corpse off in a really interesting fashion.

##### Plot Hooks: Raiders
* "Come one, come all!" Comes the cry from the traveling fighting ring, drawing the baddest Raiders from across the Wasteland.
* A new strain of chems is ravaging settlements, all pushed by a new Raider band.
* A particularly brave band of Raiders has taken to taming local Deathclaws.
* A Raider boss is building a religious following, with others spreading his gospel of bloodshed.

#### SETTLERS
Not everyone who calls the Wasteland home is a violent sociopath, just the vast majority of them. The remaining few go by many names depending on where it is they desperately try to keep calling home, but they are, one and all, Settlers. Those dedicated few who have decided to believe that even with all the evils that lurk beyond their walls, there is still some slim hope to be found in one another—in community. Attracted to the semi-standing structures of the old world, there is an unconscious irony to the idea that the new will start in the ruins of what came before. After all, despite the mold damage, pockets of radiation, and often inhospitable residents, most of the 'intact' buildings remain so, thanks to strong foundations. And if Settlers can work with anything, it's a strong foundation.

Difficult to categorize, the name Settler refers to any group of people in the Wasteland who have begun to build something new in a world that seems categorically against the mere concept of their existence. They are, above all other things, survivors, drawn together with a hope that maybe, just maybe, this time will be different, that these walls will hold, that this group of people can fend off the Raiders. Sometimes, this is the case, and the settlements flourish—becoming hubs of trade like Megaton, Diamond City, or The Hub. Others, even with the best of intentions, are torn down by famine, disease, or violence. Some simply vanish off of the face of the earth in a single evening. Hope is hope, but the Wasteland is still the Wasteland.

It is far from uncommon to find communities that are tough enough to withstand the test of time. They build themselves up and develop societies of their own, many fiercely isolationist in nature, with a distinct and clear focus on protecting their own. Others throw open their gates to newcomers, eager to become an oasis of security in the otherwise overtly hostile world beyond. Not to suggest that each and every Settler group is wholly positive; such a belief would be willingly ignorant. Some Settlers wear a face of civility like a mask and discard it as readily should the need arise. They smile one moment before planting lead into the bodies of those they just traded with. Sometimes, to survive, one must do awful things. Often, that burden falls to those in charge of a settlement, hiding the dirty secrets of their homes in dark corners so that the picture of safety can remain, so the music can keep playing—so hope can survive in this most hopeless of places.

##### Plot Hooks: Settlers
* A band of Settlers have found an excellent spot for a new settlement. Shame about the locals.
* A Settler has been working on a range of Robots that could make farm labor a thing of the past, but he needs some rare parts.
* Two rival settlements are on the edge of war over local resources.
* Every full moon for the last two months, someone in the settlement has died—another full moon is coming tomorrow.

#### SUPER MUTANTS
The Great War changed many things; nations, species, and the world as we knew it passed in a series of blossoming mushroom clouds. The sun set, and whatever America was, died. As the sun rose the next day, a new world was born alongside the light spreading across the newly christened Wasteland, full of new life, of better life. Of the Super Mutant.

While not born in that wave of nuclear energy, the Super Mutants were certainly equipped for the new world that waited for them. Naturally immune to the effects of radiation, larger, more resilient, and twice as strong as even the strongest man, Super Mutants were everything their name promised. Surprisingly, however, not all Super Mutants sprang from the same place. Yes, each of them was created from F.E.V. (Forced Evolutionary Virus), but their location and means of exposure changed from group to group, one tribe even springing up prior to the world's end in Huntersville, the unwilling victims of an experiment by West Tek. In the modern Wasteland, Super Mutants often clash with the Brotherhood of Steel, both groups eager to see the other annihilated, so there is an irony that both were effectively born in the same facility: the Mariposa Military Base. When Roger Maxson turned against the USA, sowing the seeds that would eventually grow into the Brotherhood, he was doing so in protest of the experiments he saw there, against F.E.V. So when Richard Grey arrived in 2102, beginning his long path to becoming the being that would one day be revered as The Master, it was impossible for him to know the connection that already existed between the two factions.

The Master would go on to create The Unity, his perfect vision of the future of life, the largest collection of Super Mutants ever seen in the Wasteland. In addition to their physical superiority over humans, the Super Mutants of Unity were intelligent, dangerously sophisticated, and possessed of a hive mind with their creator; some even possessed psychic abilities. In truth, The Master was successful. He had created a superior form of life. Had it not been for their inherent violence and the actions of a single Vault Dweller, the Super Mutant may well have inherited the earth. However, this was not to be the case. The Unity was destroyed, Super Mutant society shattered, their numbers hunted down and massacred by those who feared them. And so the dawn of the Super Mutant waned but did not end.

Even now, the Super Mutant is a common Wasteland sight, much to the despair of those who spot them. Though they differ in some details depending on their origin, bands of those 8ft tall hulking behemoths can be seen arrayed into war-tribes, crews, towns, or even as lone wanderers simply trying to make their way in the world. Few groups are the same beyond their rough physical description, however. Those in the Capital Wasteland, for example, are mostly of sub-human intelligence, capable of wishing for little more than bloody meat and war, whereas those in Boston are far more intelligent, with a distinct ethos of their own superiority to lesser races. Despite where you find them, Super Mutants seem to enjoy the company of their F.E.V.-influenced kin, Super Mutant Hounds, Centaurs, and Floaters, for example. In truth, though they are often appropriately viewed as savage brutes, Super Mutants are as varied and complex as any other thinking species. While those who meet one should be justifiably cautious, who are we to say what they may become?

##### Plot Hooks: Super Mutants
* Graffiti has started appearing on the outskirts of urban ruins: "Unity Rises."
* A particularly eclectic group of Mutants have been gathering people to watch them engage in some new sport.
* A new tribe has been building strength and numbers...a lot of numbers.
* A Super Mutant messenger has arrived at a known settlement carrying a letter of peace.

#### THE CULT OF THE MOTHMAN
Myths have existed long before the war and persist long after—stories passed between talkative voices around the campfire, in books and images. Myths have a power, and sometimes that power outstrips the idea of the thing—sometimes that power becomes manifest, and the myth becomes fact. Such is the case with the mysterious Mothman and the Cult that bears its name. Even before the Great War, a Cult formed in worship of the Mothman, seeing the unseen being as a herald of things yet to come. Innumerable attempts to commune with their pseudo-deity failed, with prayers and questions falling on deaf ears—but faith requires commitment. Eventually, on the eve of the Great War, a vision came to the Cult, one of fire and floods, with a warning to rise above the floods from a Red-Eyed Mothman. But, in a moment of division, a single member of the Cult also received a vision from a Purple-Eyed Mothman, one that warned to hide below ground.

This second vision caused a splintering of the Cult, with half traveling below ground and half taking to rooftops, to be promptly atomized in nuclear fire. But, as is appropriate following nuclear war, more division was guaranteed within the Cult, for below ground, in the Lucky Hole Mines they now called home, alien secrets leaked into the minds of the Cultists. Born from a darker thing that lurked deeper in the caves, those secrets fractured those who remained into two splinters: 'The Enlightened' who shunned the darker acts of those who gave into the thing below, and 'The Dim Ones' who gave themselves to a newer worship, one of darkness and blood. And so, The Enlightened fled the caves, taking themselves into the new Wasteland that awaited them, fighting through fear, doubt, and the innumerable dangers that populated the landscape. With no shortage of pain and blood, they were guided to their new home by a vision, to The Lantern, where they waited for the next sign.

Meanwhile, like a tumor beneath the flesh of Appalachia, The Dim Ones grew and prospered, their acts ascending in debasement with every passing day. So, when they crawled from the dark back to the wilds, they did so with a feverish desire to spread the word of their Red-Eyed lord. The Dim Ones spread through Appalachia, recruiting those who would be swayed and sacrificing those who wouldn't. From woodbound altars and communes, they erupt like rotten sap from a decayed tree, corrupting whatever they touch and wiping out anything that opposes the visions of their lord, a red-hued gaze dictating both life and death. But even as the wrathful lay waste, the Wise plan for the future—and look at the world with very different eyes.

##### Plot Hooks: Cult of the Mothman
* As the equinox comes, The Enlightened return and require help reclaiming their holy sites from The Dim Ones.
* In a burst of ear-piercing sonics, a purple-eyed Mothman has appeared in your settlement—what it wants is beyond you.
* A deserter has fled a nearby cult and is desperate for aid.
* Bizarre sounds have been echoing out from the Lucky Hole Mines, which have been abandoned for years...

#### THE ENCLAVE
Unlike many factions of the Wasteland, the Enclave existed before the Great War's catastrophic end, though it did so in total secrecy. An association of the rich and powerful, the Enclave was, in every way, a shadow government, guiding the actions of the USA from the background, directing it to execute its desires. And when the doomsday clock hit twelve, those who made up their ranks were already hidden safely within reinforced facilities, presidential oil rigs, and secure locations, ready to enforce their will on whatever came next. Soon after the Great War, the Enclave began to determine its new mission, but violent internal conflict sprang up as some arms of their group turned to a mission of revenge against their international enemies. Others sought instead to reaffirm their control over their own soil. Initially, movements were made to weaponize their assets in Appalachia, forcing dormant security systems to recognize domestic threats as foreign ones via the aggressive application of the Scorched Plague. This move not only threatened an extinction event but the rapid amputation of their Appalachian arm. Following this, the Enclave moved to better establish a foothold and work upon its new intent. What was once America was now a Wasteland, no longer populated with true blue patriots but with disgusting sub-human mutants.

So a singular vision came to be, reclaiming America via the complete and total extermination of all mutant life, no matter how minor the aberration—if the life wasn't Enclave, then it was already beyond saving. Since the establishment of this goal, the Enclave has come dangerously close to completion twice, once via the dispersal of a modified F.E.V. strain into the air and again into the water supply. Thankfully, for the continued existence of mutant life, both efforts were stopped dead. Each time the Enclave makes moves to enact its planned genocide of the Wasteland, the Wasteland strikes back, cutting down the Enclave and leaving them with less and less of the advanced pre-war tech that offers them an edge. Their most recent efforts in the Capital Wasteland were extremely costly, with two of their largest operational assets destroyed by the actions of a Vault Dweller and the Brotherhood of Steel. That was also the last time that the Enclave made actively visible movements within the Wasteland, with their current whereabouts, aims, and resources a mystery to everyone outside of their ranks. One thing's for sure, though, if a nuclear Armageddon wasn't enough to stop the Enclave, can anything truly call an end to their machinations? No one knows the full extent of their reach, but everyone knows the lengths they will go to to achieve their goals.

##### Plot Hooks: The Enclave
* Hidden under a seemingly abandoned ruin, you find an old military bunker. Sadly, opening the door sent a signal to an unknown location.
* An NCR squad arrives, questioning you about an Enclave escapee spotted nearby.
* Rumors of an Enclave armory are rife, and everyone wants a piece.
* An Enclave assault force comes out of nowhere and wipes out a settlement in a single night. What were they after?

#### THE INSTITUTE
Many consider The Institute to be the boogeyman of the Commonwealth. Monsters that appear in the night to abduct you, leaving a perfect replica in your place. Their mere existence sparks panic, worry, and infighting—when an agency exists that can create your exact doppelganger, how can you ever truly trust anyone? How can you believe that the person you are speaking to is the person you think you know? Your mother, brother, sister, lover, friend—are they who you think they are? Were they ever? Are you? Are your memories really yours? Can you say, with total certainty, that everything you know is real? These questions revolve around the minds of those in the Commonwealth, percolating in such a way that most are kept in a state of anxiety, which is exactly what The Institute wants.

Who The Institute is is a relatively simple question; what they want, though, is as complicated as the work they pursue on a daily basis. Simply put, The Institute is a scientific agency aimed at bringing forth technological advancement that will, in time, benefit all humanity and pull it back from the brink of extinction. To help everyone else, however, The Institute must first help itself. Beneath the ruins of the C.I.T. (Commonwealth Institute of Technology), the descendants of those first scientists who took shelter from the bombs work to develop technologies so advanced that to anyone from the outside, they may as well be magic. Teleporters, cybernetics, medicine, agricultural advancement, and, of course, their most renowned and feared creation, the Synth. It's no stretch to say that The Institute is, categorically, the most advanced faction in not just the Commonwealth but potentially the entire Wasteland. Although, with this scientific abundance, one may expect a modicum of generosity, which just isn't the case. Though The Institute has every intention of eventually returning to the surface, using the fruits of its labor to better the world, it will do so only under its own guidance when it is ready. Until that point, The Institute must remain free to pursue its goals, unlabored by outside influence. It must be able to reach out into the Wasteland, take what it needs, and return to work, regardless of who else thinks their need is greater. So, to ensure that this remains the case, they dispatch Synths to watch the Wastelanders, replacing key figures to steer the world in the way it needs to go. They eliminate threats to the only plan that matters, theirs. When others think about The Institute and see only immoral monsters, their only real failing is not being able to see the full picture to understand what is required. But that's okay; they don't need to. The Institute knows what's best.

##### Plot Hooks: The Institute
* As you uncover a particularly rare piece of salvage, an electrical snap fills your ears as metal men walk out of thin air demanding your prize.
* A murder of red-eyed crows has been spotted departing and returning to an old military base. Why?
* A member of The Institute comes to you and offers you a very important job, with a promise of entry upon completion.
* A stranger speaks a series of strange words to you, and suddenly, your memories vanish.

#### THE NEW CALIFORNIA REPUBLIC
There are few examples of successful societies in the Wasteland. Settlements are isolated instances of communities, larger factions like the Brotherhood or the Enclave more akin to dogmatic agencies than functional bastions of humanity. It takes a lot to formulate something larger than just the select few, to reestablish fair government and a functional working civilization, and there are few better examples of that of the New California Republic. From the confines of Vault 15, the inhabitants moved into the Wasteland and created the town of Shady Sands with the use of a G.E.C.K., the seedling of what would eventually grow into the NCR. Fair trade and the reestablishment of a true democracy became the pillars that held the NCR together, and within a few decades of its founding, it was the epitome of Wasteland society—an example of what could be attained by hard work, ingenuity, and the human spirit.

Their recapture of the Hoover Dam became tantamount to the success of their burgeoning state, allowing for the flow of both electricity and a spike in the quality of life experienced by those who called themselves citizens of the NCR. That, too, led to the development of their military arm, which focused on both the maintenance of internal law, the expansion into new regions, and, of course, the fighting of several wars that came to their door. Through the years, the NCR has called several factions their enemy, including the Enclave and, most famously, Caesar's Legion. When forced to military action, the NCR is heavily modeled after the army of the now-extinct American past—with battalions of soldiers ready to deploy at the President's word, alongside a scant but well-maintained Air Force, mechanized division, and the most recent addition—the Rangers. Formally brought into the NCR some years ago, the Rangers are both part of and separate from the NCR—maintaining autonomy within their own ranks while acting as a working component of the greater NCR Army. Despite their relatively small numbers, there are few sights across the Wasteland as ubiquitously known as the form of a black armored veteran Ranger. Now, the NCR stands still as a primary example of what could be a safe haven for its people, with a working government, plenty of food to go around, power, and all the security that one can expect of life within the Wasteland. Though, as with all things in this new, irradiated world, no single thing can remain constant forever. Times change, empires rise and fall, but one can bet on one factor: the Two-Headed Bear won't go anywhere without a fight.

##### Plot Hooks: New California Republic
* An NCR convoy has been hit by an unknown group. They're eager to find out who it was.
* The Rangers are on the move, en masse, and looking for aid.
* The President has put out the call for new recruits; rumor is war is coming.
* In a rare show of aggression, the NCR is forcefully annexing a large settlement for unknown reasons.

#### THE RAILROAD
When the Institute created the first Synth, they had distant ideas of what their purpose would be, menial tasks, hard labor, and the work they had no desire to do themselves. They gave little to no thought to what their work would eventually grow into, sentience, life, to some, even a soul. To them, the Synth was nothing more than another tool to be used and thrown away when its usefulness had expired or eliminated when it stopped working as planned. Anything that represented thought or feelings, just errant code, a malfunction to be removed and improved upon. Not life, no, that would be the ramblings of a bleeding heart moralist. But what if that were the case? Synths may be simulated life, but is a simulated mind not still a mind? If the statement "I think, therefore I am" is to be true, does it matter where that thought originates? Does that mind not deserve to be free? This question is what gave birth to The Railroad, a clandestine organization whose core aim is Synth liberation.

Since the discovery of the first Gen 3 Synth, the question of their independence and rights has been asked. The Railroad have a simple belief: Synths are as deserving of freedom as anyone else, and while The Institute exists, they will never know that freedom. So, unable to do anything about The Institute, they turn their assets to helping those who escape it and staying one step ahead of their creators so they can survive to continue their work. Operating on a strictly "need-to-know" basis, The Railroad has fallen victim to The Institute's relentless attempts to wipe them out on several occasions, though never to the point of total obliteration, and never without learning several important lessons from each attack. They decentralized, splitting their agents into sub-teams and keeping information compartmentalized. In effect, while many people work for The Railroad, some may never meet another member. It is, after all, the only way to maintain total safety for both the agents and the Synths they rescue. Field agents work under aliases and pick up work from dead-drops; only those within Railroad HQ have anything close to all the information, and they are hidden behind a multitude of deceptions, distractions, and, of course, should those fail—heavily armed agents.

Once a Synth is picked up, the long process of exfiltrating them from the Commonwealth starts, changing their face, wiping their memories, and dodging Institute retention teams. It's a difficult task, one that offers little in the way of thanks, no shortage of danger, and often ends with the deaths of numerous agents to save one life. But, to those who follow the Railroad, there's only one question that matters, "would you risk your life for your fellow man? Even if that man is a Synth?"

##### Plot Hooks: The Railroad
* Weeks ago, your memories vanished, and you have been hunted ever since. Someone must be able to help.
* Two strangers enter your camp. One of them is badly wounded and begs you to help their companion get to a specific location.
* One of your old scavenging sites has a series of strange holotapes with locations and codewords hidden inside. They weren't there last time you checked.
* Someone has offered you a lot of caps to hunt down and kill an escaped Synth, regardless of their protectors.

#### VAULT DWELLERS
Before the bombs fell, the world was doing little more than waiting for them to fall. Some tried to ignore it, to pretend that maybe, just maybe, it was all just going to blow over before the worst happened. Others prepared, digging out bomb shelters and hoarding cans of food, trying to guess what life after atomic Armageddon may look like. But the worst not only prepared for the bombs to drop, they encouraged them and happily prepared their quarterly reports. The Vault-Tec Corporation ("The first and last name in Vault technology") was, among other things, the American answer to post-war society, their ace-in-the-hole for the worst-case scenario. But of course, companies exist to make money, and how do you make money when the world is going to end? Well, you make the consumer the commodity. And it was that mentality that shaped the roll-out of the Vaults, their experiments, and, of course, the lives of those who lived within them.

And so, when the bombs did fall, and people fled to their blue and yellow-painted sanctuaries, they had every hope that safety was all that awaited them. Sadly, for the vast majority of those trusting Americans, this was not the case. Now, locked within commercially viable sarcophagi, the 'Vault Dwellers' became suddenly exposed, knowingly or unknowingly, to numerous 'tests,' each concocted by a selection of interested individuals, each with their own intent to see just what could happen when people are exposed to whatever situation they could dream up. What happens if a selection of chem addicts are 'cured' and then randomly reintroduced to their vice? What happens if a community is faced with sudden mechanical failure, forcing them to venture into the irradiated surface? What if there aren't enough men or women? Or one man was cloned over and over again? Or the air was replaced with a chemical agent? Just some of the possibilities, some of the 'tests' administered by people who designed them, knowing they may never see the outcome.

These days, even though most Vaults lie undisturbed, no small number have had their door slide open to let the thick, rad-laden air of the surface seep through and let their residents step free. However, the question always remains, how did what happened within the Vault affect those leaving it? What twisted mass sits behind their eyes—madness and violence? A spark of hope and the makings of heroism? Or simply a mind desperate to return to the taciturn safety of their industrial home? The answers are as varied as the Vaults themselves and will never match the answer before.

* One Vault-Tec rep still wanders the Wasteland, looking for new residents, but why?
* A particularly vile Vault experiment has breached the door and is rapidly spreading.
* A Vault has opened for the first time in centuries, and word has reached the local Raiders...

### THE STORY

As you read this book, it pays to bear in mind what the key themes of the Fallout universe are and how your story in it will reflect that. Each tale in the Wasteland matters, at least to those it directly involves, and often has far-reaching implications that many wouldn't even consider until those implications turn up at the door with a bad attitude and a laser rifle. As mentioned earlier in this chapter, every person, mutant, or monster you encounter in your quests will be part of the story, a unique character that will have their own wants and needs (even if those needs are as simple as wanting to eat you!). The same is true for the Wasteland itself; the varied spaces from the east coast to the west coast are all different, and those locales will be as much as a character as any Power Armor-clad Paladin of the Brotherhood of Steel.

#### CHOICE
It was said that 'choices are the hinges of destiny,' and in few stories is that more true than Fallout. In the past, the players of Fallout games have had to make choices, from those that seemed nearly pointless to those that were nearly impossible to make. Buy bullets or Stimpaks? Help a stranger or rob them? Blow up Megaton for the money, or spare it for morality? Every choice matters in Fallout, and there will always be consequences—immediately or long down the road, they will happen. So as you chart out your own story, remember that facet—that you will have to make choices and, more often than not, no one option is better than the other. Maybe taking the moral high ground means sacrificing food, water, or gear that would otherwise make it easier to see another sunrise. A clearly 'evil' action to a few could save dozens elsewhere. So think to yourself, what ripples do your actions bring to the NPCs of your particular world? And how exactly are those actions going to come back and bite you?

#### CIRCLES
"War, war never changes." This maxim is known to every fan of the Fallout series, be they a diehard from game one or a fresh-faced enthusiast who watched the Amazon Series. It clearly demonstrates one of Fallout's greatest themes; circles, loops, or simple repetition, how, in essence, the more things change, the more they stay the same. In 2077, the human race had driven itself into such a state of rampant greed and paranoia that, in the space of an hour, they nearly wiped themselves out. Billions died in blossoming clouds of nuclear fury. You would hope that perhaps the few who survived would have learnt from that. But every day in the Wasteland proves that greed, paranoia, and hate are still common pastimes and that no matter what, war will always come. What does that mean for your story? Will you accept the cycle of violence or try and reject it? And will that choice even matter?

In addition to that, we see even more loops in the world of Fallout, ideas that are commonplace in our modern world reappearing long after the apocalypse albeit twisted. Caesar's Legion adopts the viewpoints of ancient Rome, while the NCR attempts to rebuild modern democracy. The new world is built up in the ruins of the old, often rebuilt as it was rather than as something new. In so many places, time and society repeat and Fallout implies that this will likely never change.

#### IRONY AND DARK HUMOR
Fallout has always strived to hold up the mirror and laugh at the absurdity of the world. It finds fun in the hopeless environment of the Wasteland, where wit as dry as the soil often pokes and jabs at current world issues through the veil of fiction. Most of the humor of Fallout is observational, reflective of both the human condition and state of the world we live in—playing plain old human arrogance against the actions we undertake—laughing at the absurdity of it all. Advertisements for Nuka-Cola and anti-communist propaganda are more common than books, and the rampant consumerism of pre-war America hasn't vanished after the apocalypse; we just changed what we were consuming. So remember in your own stories that there is doom and gloom around every corner of the Wasteland, but there's always something to smile about, even in the darkest spot.

#### AFTER THE WORST
Say what you will about the Wasteland, but technically, the worst has already happened. Fallout starts with the apocalypse—and everything that follows after can't be any worse than Armageddon, surely? Fallout has us ask what comes after the end of the world once the dust has settled and we have been given the opportunity to learn from our mistakes. As we discussed earlier, often, this is where we see the circles creep in and repetition. But what does the worst mean not for humanity but for the individual? Who do we become as we transition from one world state to the next? Will we be better for the end of days or worse? Only you can answer that question.
